\chapter{Marsupials}\label{ch:Mar}
Looking at previous studies in the region,
in \srf{01-05} we noted that \citet{Blust1982,Blust2012b}
saw marsupials and the terms associated with them as having
important subgrouping value for the Austronesian languages of eastern Indonesia.
He argued that two etyma,
which he reconstructed as **kandoRa ‘cuscus’ (see \frf{14-01})
and **mans(aə)r ‘bandicoot’ (see \frf{14-02}),
 are not found in languages west of the Blust line,
but are found in both EMP languages
and CMP languages, thus justifying the higher level CEMP subgroup
that includes both of those.
His later assessment \citep[727]{Blust2013} of the significance of
these two etyma was summarised saying,
“the distribution of cognate sets for marsupial mammals must
be considered the jewel in the crown for this [CEMP] hypothesis”.

\footnotetext{The two pictures are not to the same scale here.
Having accompanied Buru men hunting cuscus many times,
Grimes can verify that the adult grey cuscus can average 30--60cm
in body length with an additional 30--60cm of tail length.
Spotted cuscus and bear cuscus are said to be larger.
Various bandicoot species are said to range from 28cm-80cm in length.
Presumably this includes their tails.}

Hence, we devote an entire chapter to try to understand his reasoning,
as well as to understand some potentially insurmountable problems
with these terms and their implications for subgrouping.
In \srf{14-01} we look at the distribution of certain marsupials
across biogeographical Wallacea to provide context for the terms
used to describe them,
discussed in subsequent sections.
In \srf{14-02} we discuss various methodological issues relating
to subgrouping, and try to identify the assumptions behind
\citeauthor{Blust2012b}’s (\citeyear{Blust2012b}) resistance
to revising the more significant claims of his (\citeyear{Blust1982}) work
in the face of reasonable criticisms by \citet{Schapper2011}.
In \srf{14-03} we look at **kandoRa
and incrementally related terms stretching from Sulawesi through Halmahera to Papua.
In \srf{14-04}, we look at **mans(aə)r (which we reconstruct
as **mantəR), its glosses, form, and distribution.
In \srf{14-05} we look at the distribution of Wallacean subgroups
that have verifiable words glossed ‘bandicoot’.
And in \srf{14-06} we look at higher level implications of this
more complete picture of marsupials in Wallacea.
