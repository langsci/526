\sscp{\tsc{Kisar-Luang}}{04-06-02}
A node containing Roma,
Kisar, and the Luang cluster is well defined on the basis of
*z > **ty [ʧ] which is not only unique within the region,
but almost unique among all Malayo-Polynesian languages.\footnote{
		The nearest languages with *z > \it{ʧ} are in Borneo \citep[279]{Smith2017a}.}
We call this node \tsc{Kisar-Luang} after the two most socially
prominent islands on which these languages are spoken.
As discussed above, the change *z > **ty [ʧ] may provide evidence
for a more extended node within Southwest Maluku also including \tsc{Teun-Nila-Serua} and \tsc{Babar}.
Additional evidence for \tsc{Kisar-Luang} comes from shared *s
> \it{h, s}; *j > \it{r}; *aw > \it{a};
and *ŋ > \it{n} in most positions,
of which *j > \it{r}
and *-aw > \it{a} also occur widely among \tsc{Southwest Maluku} languages.

The strongest evidence for \tsc{Kisar-Luang} comes from the reflexes
of *z, which are unique in the region.
\citet[289f]{Mills2010} provides a discussion of *z in these languages.
Roma appears to have *z > \it{ty} [ʧ] before non-front vowels,
%and *z > \it{t} elsewhere (before 
and *z > \it{t} medially with raising of the following vowel to \it{i}.
The Luang languages/dialects have *z > \it{t}
and Kisar mergers *z/*t > \it{k}.
These changes can be accounted for by positing Proto-Kisar-Luang
*z > **ty [ʧ] with subsequent **ty > **t in Kisar
and Luang, and **t > \it{k} in Kisar.
The reflexes of *z in are given in \trf{04-27}.
(Throughout this section Wetan from Teun Island [\citealp{Chlenova2006}]
is given as “Wetan (T)”
and Wetan from Wetan Island
[\citealp{DeJosselinDeJong1987}] is given
as “Wetan (W)” or just “Wetan”.)

Another change which provides evidence for \tsc{Kisar-Luang}
is *s > \it{h}.
This change is not complete in any language.
In particular, Leti does not participate in this change
and Moa only has *s > \it{h} intervocalically or before another consonant.
There are also sporadic cases of *s = \it{s} in other languages.
Examples of *s > \it{h,
s} are given in \trf{04-28}.
Wetan has subsequent **h > {\0}
and Luang also has **h > {\0} in at least one word.

while other descriptions tend to describe it as a glide.

[{\ldots}] with less lip rounding
and more friction than the English [w]”
(\citealp[9]{TaberTaber2015}, \citealp[34]{Steven1991}),
with allophones [ʋ] and [v] depending on adjacent vowels.
Reflexes are given in \trf{04-31}.

Within the \tsc{Kisar-Luang} node,
Kisar and Roma can be placed together based on shared *wa > \it{o}
in reflexes of *wahiyəR ‘water’
and *w > {\0} in other identified reflexes,
though Kisar retains *w in *wani > \it{wani} ‘bee’.
Additional evidence for \tsc{Kisar-Roma} comes from shared *ma-p
> \it{m} in reflexes of *ma-panas ‘hot’ as opposed to *ma-p >
\it{p} in the other languages (see \trf{04-28})
and *j > \it{ll} (rather than *j  > \it{r}) in reflexes of *huaji ‘younger sibling’.
Against this evidence for \tsc{Kisar-Roma},
is shared *ay > \it{i} in Kisar
and the Luang languages/dialects,
while Roma has *ay > \it{a}.
Examples of the reflexes of *w
and *ay in the \tsc{Kisar-Luang} languages are shown in \trf{04-33}.
