\chapter{Timor-Babar}\label{ch:TB}
There is phonological support for a \tsc{Timor-Babar} subgroup
which contains most Austronesian languages of the Greater Timor region.
Centred on Timor, this includes Rote Island to its immediate
southwest, Atauro to the north
and most islands of the geographic region of southwest Maluku
encompassing Wetar in the west,
Serua in the north,
and southwest Tanimbar in the east.
%Seluwasan and Makatian of southwest Tanimbar also belong to this subgroup.
The only Austronesian languages of this region which are excluded
from \tsc{Timor-Babar} are the \tsc{Central Timor} languages
(Welaun, Kemak, Tokodede, and \tsc{Mambae}; see \Crf{ch:CT}).
A map showing the languages which belong to the \tsc{Timor-Babar}
subgroup is given in \frf{04-01}.

\tsc{Timor-Babar} is defined by the change *p > **h word initially
and medially (see discussion below for the reflexes of *p word finally).
Proto-Timor-Babar **h was then often lost.
The reflexes of PMP *p in six cognate sets in most \tsc{Timor-Babar}
languages are shown in \trf{04-01}.

Within \tsc{Timor-Babar} we identify eight nodes: \tsc{Helong}
(\srf{04-01}), \tsc{Rote-Meto} (\srf{04-02}), \tsc{Eastern Timor} (\srf{04-03}),
\tsc{Lakalei-Idate} (\srf{04-04}), \tsc{Wetar-Atauro} (\srf{04-05}), \tsc{Southwest Maluku} (\srf{04-06}),
and \tsc{Southwest Tanimbar} (\srf{04-07}).
A complete classification of the \tsc{Timor-Babar} languages
along with all subordinate nodes is given in \srf{04-08}.

The only languages which do not show *p > \it{h},
{\0} in all words in \trf{04-01} are some of the Rote languages
(Tii, Termanu, Dela-Oenale) and Fehan Tetun which have *p > \it{ʔ} word medially.
However, in both cases the most closely related languages (Meto
and East Tetun respectively) show *p > \it{h} word medially
and it is thus reasonable to propose *p > **h > ʔ in these words.\footnote{
		An alternate scenario, raised by a reviewer,
		would be to propose that the glottal stop in these forms is conservative
		with *p > **ʔ being the initial sound change,
		followed by **ʔ > \it{h}, {\0}.
		While this is possible,
		simplicity (**h > \it{ʔ} in two branches vs.
		**ʔ > \it{h} in five branches)
		and areal patterns favour the pathway *p > **f > **h.}

Reflexes of initial *ma-p must be treated separately to those
of *p as most \tsc{Timor-Babar} languages have *ma-p > \it{p}
or *ma-p > \it{b},
probably as a result of an intermediate nasal-stop cluster stage, **mp and/or **mb.

The only cases in which *p > \it{h,} {\0} did not occur is word
finally in \tsc{Southwest Tanimbar} which has *-p = \it{p} or
*-p > \it{f} /{\gap}{\#} (see \srf{04-07} for details) as well
as at least three examples of final *p > \it{s} in other \tsc{Timor-Babar}
languages, given in \qf{04-01}--\qf{04-03} below.\footnote{
		Many \tsc{Rote-Meto} languages have
		\it{-s} as a nominal suffix (\citealp[442f, 445f]{Edwards2020}, \citealp[167--176]{Tamelan2021}), but that does
		not account for all cases of putative *p > \it{s} in these languages
		\citep[74]{Edwards2021}.}
In the case of *(ma)-qudip ‘alive’
and **malip ‘laugh’, all languages have multiple reflexes some
of which show *p > \it{s}
and some of which show *p > {\0}.
All other \tsc{Timor-Babar} languages for which reflexes of these
words have been identified have final *p > {\0}.

The final vowel of all three words is *i.
Thus, this change may be explicable as a case of assimilation
of earlier **h the place of the previous high-front vowel.
The full pathway may have been *p > **h > **ç > \it{s}.
If this hypothesis is accepted,
these instances of final *p would be consistent with the change
of *p > \it{h,} {\0} attested in \tsc{Timor-Babar} languages
in other word positions.

The change *p > **h is cross-linguistically well attested.
However, this sound change does not occur in other subgroups
of the region and is thus exclusive to \tsc{Timor-Babar}.
\tsc{Bima-Lembata}, \tsc{Central Timor}, \tsc{Sula-Buru},
and \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} retain *p = **p,
while \tsc{Aru} has *p > **ɸ (\crf{ch:Aru})
and \tsc{Ambon-Seram} has *p > **f (\crf{ch:AS}).

From a cross linguistic perspective,
it is likely that \tsc{Timor-Babar} *p > **h went through a labial fricative stage.
Direct evidence for this comes from Selaru word-final *p > \it{f, h} (see \srf{04-07}).
The change of *p to a voiceless labial fricative is also found
in \tsc{Aru}, and \tsc{Ambon-Seram}.
Given this, it may provide weak phonological evidence for uniting
these groups together within Malayo-Polynesian.
Nonetheless, even if such a group is proposed,
\tsc{Timor-Babar} would remain a distinct node on the basis of
subsequent **f > \it{h,} {\0} word initially and medially.
See \srf{13-01-02} for more discussion of this possibility.

PMP *ŋ undergoes change in most \tsc{Timor-Babar} languages,
usually through *ŋ/*n > \it{n}.
However, *ŋ is retained unchanged in \tsc{Helong} (\srf{04-01})
and \tsc{Hresuk-Raklungu} (\srf{04-05}) Some other languages
have changes other than *ŋ > \it{n},
such as most \tsc{Peripheral Babar} languages (\srf{04-06-05-01})
which have *ŋ > **ʔ > {\0}.
Even among languages which have *ŋ > \it{n} in most words,
there are often traces of *ŋ in other words.
Thus, for instance, \citet{Mills2010} points out that most members
of the \tsc{Kisar-Luang} node (\srf{04-06-02}) of \tsc{Southwest
Maluku} have *ŋ > \it{n} in all environments except for the Luang
language/dialect cluster which has *ŋ > \it{k} /{\gap}\it{r}.
An example is *saŋəlaR > **səŋaR > **seŋrə > Leti \it{sekra},
Luang \it{hekrə} ‘fry’, but Kisar \it{harna} ‘burn’ (with metathesis
of **nr > \it{rn}).
Similarly, \citet{Mills2010} draws attention to PMP *laŋit >
Teun, Nila, Serua \it{lakti}, while the Luang language/dialect cluster
has *ŋ > \it{n} in this word; e.g. Luang \it{lyanti} ‘sky’.
To account for these reflexes we would need to reconstruct *ŋ
in such words for Proto-Southwest Maluku.

A similar situation exists in the \tsc{Rote-Meto} languages.
While *ŋ > \it{n} has occurred in about two thirds (21/33) of
known PMP words with *ŋ,
there are exceptions which require reconstruction of Proto-Rote-Meto **ŋ or **ŋg.
Thus, for instance, PMP *ŋilu ‘sour’ is reflected in the Rote
languages as \it{ni{\tl}nilu} ‘tamarind’,
most \tsc{Meto} \it{maiʔninuʔ} ‘sour’,
but Kusa-Manea \it{ka{\tl}kinu} ‘sour’ thus requiring reconstruction of Proto-Rote-Meto **ŋilu.
Similar examples include PMP \mbox{*ma-diŋdiŋ} > Proto-Rote-Meto
**ma-(ka)-riŋin ‘cold’, PMP *ŋuda > Proto-Rote-Meto **ŋura ‘young of plants’,
and PMP *saŋəlaR > Proto-Rote-Meto **seŋa ‘fry’.
See \citet{Edwards2021} for details.

\newpage
Nonetheless, some cognate sets do show *ŋ > \it{n}
in all \tsc{Timor-Babar} languages.
Two likely examples are reflexes of *haŋin ‘wind’ and *dəŋəR ‘hear’.
One possible explanation is that *ŋ > \it{n} began to diffuse
at an early stage between members of the \tsc{Timor-Babar} family,
probably between ancestral stages of the current languages.
Thus, while *ŋ > \it{n} cannot be used to define \tsc{Timor-Babar},
it does have some use as supporting evidence for low-level nodes within \tsc{Timor-Babar}.
