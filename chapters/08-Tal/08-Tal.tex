\chapter{Taliabu}\label{ch:Tal}
The Sula archipelago (Indonesian \it{kepulauan Sula},
which includes the islands of Sulabesi,
Mangole and Taliabu and some small satellite islands),
was once part of the 16th century battles to control the spice
trade between the Portuguese,
the Spanish, the Dutch,
the English, the Makassarese, and the Javanese.
It was important enough to have a large fort (see \frf{08-01}).
The Sula Islands and Buru came under the political domination
of Ternate over several centuries.
Loan words from Ternate,
a Papuan language of North Halmahera,
are found in all of these languages,
but to a much greater degree in the \tsc{Taliabu} languages (\srf{02-02-02}).
Fortgens, in the Holle lists \citep{Stokhof1980b} identified
52 loans from Ternate in Soboyo,
and 43 loans from Ternate in Kadai.
But in the intervening centuries these islands have been quite
marginalised, and peripheral to major trade routes and politics.
In modern Indonesia, they continue to be connected distantly
to Ternate in the north,
rather than to Ambon.
Regarding the languages to their west in Sulawesi,
\citet[65]{Mead2003b} notes “Formerly the Banggai held sway over
the Saluan and Balantak regions and not the other way around.” So in the
context of the discussion in this chapter,
the socio-political dynamics in Banggai were very different from the Sula archipelago.
Banggai was in a dominant role,
whereas the Sula Islands were in a subordinate role.

\citet{Stresemann1927} excluded the languages of the Sula archipelago
from his Proto-Ambon subgroup (roughly Buru
and Ambelau plus \tsc{Ambon-Seram}), as did \citet{Chlenov1976}.
\citet{Blust1981} linked Soboyo with Buru.
\citet{Collins1981} linked Buru, Sula,
Mangole, Taliabu, and Ambelau.
After review, the basis in \citet{Blust1981}
and \citet{Collins1981} for linking these languages is mostly
on the strength of *R > \it{h}.

At a higher level Collins links Buru-Sula-Taliabu
and Ambelau with \tsc{Ambon-Seram},
although after updating the innovations that define Collins’
(1981:40) Proto-Central Maluku to modern understanding of PMP
there are five retentions,
two non-exclusive mergers, and one error.
Previous subgrouping arguments linking these languages based
on mergers of outdated proto-phonemes *-əy/*-ay,
*Z/z, *D/*d, and *r/*R \citep{Collins1981} must be set aside
as not exclusive, no longer reflecting current understanding of Austronesian historical
phonology, and therefore having little value for subgrouping (see \crf{ch:CM},
footnotes 11 and 15).\footnote{
		Based on modern understanding,
		these are retentions, not innovations.
		\citet[575--577]{Blust2013} does have *D (identified as retroflex)
		as different from *d,
		but only in word-final position,
		and only distinct in some languages of western Indonesia,
		so the distinction is not relevant here.
		Most of the words \citet{Collins1981} attributes to *r are more
		recently attributed to *R \citep{BlustTrussel2020},
		except for *ma-bu(Rr)it ‘behind’,
		which remains unclear, so \citeauthor{Collins1981}’ use of the two symbols is
		more of an orthographic issue than a merger of present day PMP
		*r/*R.}
\citet[48]{Collins1983} says “Taliabo is closely related to Buru”.
Yet \citet{Bloyd2015,Bloyd2020} does not include the \tsc{Taliabu} data
in his reconstruction of “Proto-Sula”.
\citet[53]{Bloyd2020} observes “Sula’s precise genetic subgrouping remains unsettled”.

Both \citet{Blust1981} and \citet{Collins1981} noted that the
behaviour of the PMP historical vowel-glide sequences *-ay,
*-aw in Soboyo and Taliabu is significantly different from all
other languages in central Maluku,
and as \citet[48]{Collins1983} says,
“indicates late retention of the complete diphthong”.
Another feature that perhaps sets \tsc{Taliabu} apart is the
lexically specific retention of PMP *h = \it{h} \citep{Blust1981}.
How do we unravel these conflicting views? Part of the competing
views above stem from \it{assuming} that the \tsc{Taliabu} languages
group with Sanana-Mangole because of proximity.

See the location of the subgroups across central Maluku in \frf{07-01},
and the location of individual languages in Figures \ref{fig:07-03} and \ref{fig:08-02}.
The discussion in this present chapter assumes an awareness of
the broader issues in \crf{ch:CM}.

The dilemma with \tsc{Taliabu}\footnote{
		The \tsc{Taliabu} node
		has several varieties whose status as languages or dialects remains unclear.
		There are different historical sound correspondences,
		different morphology, and differences in the lexicon.
		Soboyo and Kadai are recorded as having many differences in \citet{Fortgens1921}.
		That is the primary Soboyo source for \citet{Blust1981}.
		\citet{Collins1981,Collins1989} provides additional data for Taliabu
		and Kadai, along with some Sanana data.
		\citet{Stokhof1980b} provides data for “Sobojo”,
		Taliabu, Kadai, “Mangoli”, and Sanana (Fagudu).
		Fortgens is attributed as the source of the data in Stokhof for
		Soboyo and Kadai, and it is often quite detailed with additional notes.
		Fortgens documents Taliabu as distinct from Soboyo
		and Kadai in \citet{Stokhof1980b}.
		\citet{Bloyd2015,Bloyd2020} provides additional data for Sanana
		and Mangole, but tellingly does not include \tsc{Taliabu} data
		in his reconstruction of Proto-Sula.
		The Soboyo, Taliabu, and Kadai languages cluster on
		and around the island of Taliabu,
		hence our name for the node.}
is that it appears to have multiple
allegiances: it shares some features with \tsc{Sula-Buru} (to
the southeast) and others with \tsc{Celebic} (to the west).
However, the features shared between \tsc{Taliabu}
and \tsc{Sula-Buru} are more easily attributable to parallel
development (*R > \it{h}) or  contact than those shared between
\tsc{Taliabu} and \tsc{Celebic}.
We thus propose that \tsc{Taliabu} subgroups with \tsc{Celebic}
(specifically within the \tsc{Eastern Celebic} node),
with parallel development and contact effects with \tsc{Sula-Buru}.
The list below summarises the relevant changes,
also tabulated in \trf{08-01}, and represented in a wave diagram in \frf{08-00}.
Discussion follows.

\begin{itemize}
	\item PMP *-ay > \it{e},
				*-aw > \it{o} is shared with \tsc{Celebic} languages to the west,
				and not with \tsc{Sula-Buru} (or \tsc{Ambon-Seram}) languages.
	\item PMP *j > \it{y} found in \tsc{Taliabu} is also a feature of \tsc{Celebic} languages.
				There are two lexically specific cases of *j > \it{l,} one of
				which is shared with both \tsc{Sula-Buru} and \tsc{Ambon-Seram}.
	\item PMP *ə > \it{o} is shared with \tsc{Eastern Celebic} languages,
				and not with \tsc{Sula-Buru} (or \tsc{Ambon-Seram}) languages.
	\item PMP *awa > \it{oa} is shared with \tsc{Eastern Celebic} languages.
	\item PMP *R > \it{h} is shared with \tsc{Sula-Buru}.
				(*R > \it{h} is also found in Tukang Besi of Southeast Sulawesi,
				and west of Buru.)
	\item PMP *y/*z > **y is shared with \tsc{Nuclear Sula-Buru},
				but not Ambelau, where *z > \it{l}.\footnote{
				*y = **y also occurs
				in \tsc{Saluan-Banggai} (a branch of \tsc{Eastern Celebic}),
				but reflexes of *z are different in \tsc{Saluan-Banggai} (\it{d}, \it{ʤ}, \it{s}).
				\citet[71, 76f]{Mead2003b} shows that within \tsc{Saluan-Banggai},
				PMP *y/*j > **y,
				is preserved as \it{y} in Banggai,
				and “weakened” in the other languages.}
	\item Other features of the lexicon
				and morphosyntax are shared with Sanana
				and Mangole, but not with Banggai, Buru or Ambelau.
\end{itemize}

Ultimately, it will require experts in \tsc{Celebic} to decide
whether the shared innovations given above make a convincing
case for the inclusion of \tsc{Taliabu}.
From our perspective, the historical phonology of \tsc{Taliabu}
is quite different to languages to its east,
as the wave diagrams in \frf{07-04}
and \frf{07-05} make clear.
If more research indicates that \tsc{Taliabu} should not be included
in \tsc{Celebic}, it seems better to treat it as an isolate than to place it with \tsc{Sula-Buru}.

It is also important to note the reflexes of *z > \it{l} in Ambelau
(see \srf{09-02} for more discussion) are different to both \tsc{Nuclear Sula-Buru} and \tsc{Taliabu}.
This means that if \tsc{Taliabu} were grouped with \tsc{Sula-Buru}
on the basis of shared *z/*y > **y,
the developments of *-ay,
*-aw, *ə, and *j which are shared between \tsc{Nuclear Sula-Buru}
and Ambelau, and shared in a different way between \tsc{Celebic}
and \tsc{Taliabu}, would have to be treated as parallel developments
and/or as spread by diffusion.
That is, two separate cases of parallel development/diffusion
affecting four segments (eight separate changes).
On the other hand,
under our proposal we only have to propose one case of parallel
development/diffusion affecting two segments: *R > \it{h}
and *z > **y.
For the moment, linking \tsc{Taliabu} with
\tsc{Celebic} seems the most reasonable
solution on the available data.
