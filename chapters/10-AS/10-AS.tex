\chapter{Ambon-Seram}\label{ch:AS}
In this chapter we discuss the \tsc{Ambon-Seram} subgroup,
which includes the languages of the western three quarters of
Seram (excluding Bobot and languages east of it),
all of Ambon-Lease, languages in the Manipa Strait between Seram
and Buru, and the recently extinct Kayeli language of eastern Buru.
The location of these languages is given in \frf{07-03}.
The discussion in this chapter assumes an awareness of the broader
issues across the region discussed in \crf{ch:CM}.

Languages of \tsc{Ambon-Seram} are some of the most poorly documented
languages of Wallacea, and for many languages the only available data are 19th century
word lists, some of which are of questionable quality.
For several languages the only available data are words presented
by \citet{Stresemann1927} and \citet{Collins1983} in their classifications of the languages of this region.
The situation is compounded by wide-scale language endangerment
with several languages having become extinct recently.
These factors mean that we are not in a position to discuss in
detail the internal subgrouping of \tsc{Ambon-Seram}.
Instead, our discussion focusses primarily on issues which may
shed light on the higher level branches of \tsc{Ambon-Seram}.

The main defining sound change that sets \tsc{Ambon-Seram} apart
from neighbouring subgroups (apart from Banda) is the merger
of *n/*ŋ > **n (\srf{07-04-01-01}).
\tsc{Ambon-Seram} languages also share *-ay > **a
and *p > **f,
though these two changes are found more widely across the region
(*p > **f does not occur in \tsc{Sula-Buru},
and Kur of \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} preserves *p = \it{p} [\srf{11-04}]).
All \tsc{Ambon-Seram} languages except \tsc{Seti} also share *mb/*mp > **mp.
In addition to these sound changes,
there are partially overlapping mergers involving *d,
*z, *R, *l, and *j which affect different
combinations of \tsc{Ambon-Seram} languages.
\frf{10-01} shows the branches of \tsc{Ambon-Seram} to which specific
discussion is devoted within this chapter.
(A full classification is given in \srf{10-04}.)

We begin in \srf{10-01} with a discussion of the partially overlapping
merger of *d/*z/*R/*l/*j > **l which affects many \tsc{Ambon-Seram}
languages and the subsequent splits which affect the outcome of this merger.
This includes specific discussion of the recently extinct Kayeli
language of Buru Island in \srf{10-01-01}
and shows that, despite its geographic location,
it is indeed an \tsc{Ambon-Seram} language,
though with significant contact effects from \tsc{Sula-Buru}.
We also discuss complexities of the \tsc{East Littoral} node
in \srf{10-01-02} regarding the reflexes of *d/*z/*R/*l/*j.

In \srf{10-02} we discuss the \tsc{Northwest Seram} node (including Alune
and closely related languages) which is conservative in several
respects, including preservation of final *-aw as \it{-akwe} in some words.
We then discuss in \srf{10-03} languages which do not show a
full merger of *d/*z/*R/*l/*j,
but instead merge a subset of these segments or only have a partial merger.
In \srf{10-04} we give a detailed classification of the \tsc{Ambon-Seram} languages.
