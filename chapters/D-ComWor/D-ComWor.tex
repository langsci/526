\chapter{Comparative word list}\label{ch:ComWor}
In this appendix we provide a 494-item word list designed to help
collect data useful for comparative purposes for
languages of eastern Indonesian and Timor-Leste.
Many Swadesh-style basic word lists contain concepts which are
not relevant to this region (e.g.
‘snow’, ‘ice’) and/or do not include concepts which are important
in this region (e.g.
‘house’, ‘cuscus’, ‘maternal uncle’).
Likewise, elicitation of a standard Swadesh basic word list does
not always yield sufficient exemplars of certain PMP proto-phonemes.

This list has been designed to capture both a range of culturally
important concepts, and words which are likely to be stable reflexes of PMP etyma,
particularly of words which contain uncommon PMP proto-phonemes.
Thus, for instance, ‘foam’
and ‘gall, bile’ are included, as these are often reflexes of
*bujəq and *qapəju respectively and can provide additional data on the
reflexes of PMP *j (and possibly *q or *əq),
which in turn can provide extremely helpful information on the
changes affecting that proto-phoneme in a particular language.
(See, for instance, the discussion of the position of the \tsc{Eastern
Bomberai} languages in \srf{11-05-02-01}).

Experience in the field shows that it takes about three
hours to fill in this list with a group of speakers.
Of the 494 items in this list,
313 (marked with an asterisk in the left-most column) have been
identified as essential/core items that should be collected for
a language when time is limited or the situation does not allow
collection of all 494 items.
The reduced 313-item list takea about one and a half hours
to fill in in and is about the maximum
that can be collected in a single sitting before the concentration
of the linguist and consultant(s) starts to flag.
Given this, if the goal is to fill in the entire 494 item list,
it makes sense to do this in two sessions with a break in between.

Printable versions for use in the field of the 494-item word list
(with notes in Indonesian),
as well as a spreadsheet version,
can be downloaded from \textcolor{red}{\tbf{XXX}}.
Consider sending a copy of your word list(s) to the authors for
use in further comparative studies.
(Please also note the use of IPA characters to ensure we do not
introduce additional errors.) 
The collection of this word list should also be recorded,
subject to the consent of the consultants.
Doing so may provide useful insights later on when more things are better understood.
Similarly, if the investigator is not sure what elaboration is
being given by the native speaker for a given item, they should
write down/record as much of what is said in the language given,
rather than translating or interpreting what is said at the time.
Particularly for the longer 494-item version of the word list,
an additional recording with key consultants can be made after
completion of the word list as a second pass to produce a cleaner recording.

Material in parentheses is to help reduce
ambiguities and get more consistent responses for comparison.
So for {\#}116 \it{(laut) teduh} ‘calm (of sea)’,
one would say “teduh” first to indicate what is targeted,
immediately followed before they respond by “laut teduh” to clarify
what kind of \it{teduh}.
Similarly {\#}363 \it{jalan (setapak)} ‘path (step)’,
one would say “jalan {\ldots} jalan setapak” to indicate we are
looking for the noun ‘path’,
and not the verb ‘go, walk’.
If they respond with the fuller information that includes what
is in parentheses, then the fuller information is what is written down.
Such clarifications are based on extensive experience collecting word lists.

The notes column contains scientific names of flora
and fauna, additional guides on elicitation,
as well as Kupang Malay (K:)
and Maluku/Ambon Malay (A:) equivalents of the standard Indonesian concepts.
