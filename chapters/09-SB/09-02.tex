\scp{Ambelau}{09-02}
Ambelau forms a branch within \tsc{Sula-Buru} that is separate from \tsc{Nuclear Sula-Buru}.
Many Ambelau speakers get their water
and till their crops on the Buru mainland around Wae Tawa
and are bilingual in the Wae Sama dialect of Buru.
Very few Buru speakers have any command of the Ambelau language.
During the VOC era,
Kayeli was the dominant focus politically
and economically over Buru and Ambelau.

Ambelau groups with \tsc{Sula-Buru} on the basis of *a/*-ay/*-aw
> \it{a} (see \trf{08-02}, \trf{07-03}, as well as
*mat\tbr{ay} > \it{mar\tbr{a}} ‘dead’,
*qu\tbr{ay} > \it{u\tbr{a}} ‘rattan’,
*lin\tbr{aw} > \it{eɲ-lin\tbr{a}} ‘\tsc{3sg}-calm (water)’);
*ə > \it{e}
(see \trf{09-01}); *R > \it{h} (see \trf{07-01}
and \trf{08-11}); *q/*h > {\0} in all positions;
and the lexically specific split of *j > \it{l},
{\0} described in \srf{07-03-02}
and \srf{07-04-02-01} (see \trf{07-05} and \trf{07-12}).

Ambelau also shares with \tsc{Sula-Buru} the lexically specific
innovations of: *kahiw > **kau > \it{ʔau-a} ‘wood,
tree’, *waŋka > \it{waʔa} ‘boat’,
*mata > **dama > \it{lama-ni} ‘eye’,
**biak > \it{bia-e} ‘banana’ (see \trf{07-13}),
*əpat > \it{faa} ‘four’,
and *ənəm > \it{nee} ‘six’ (see \trf{07-14} and \trf{07-15}).

While Ambelau clearly belongs with \tsc{Sula-Buru},
it shares some features with \tsc{Ambon-Seram}.
Such features include: 

\begin{itemize}
	\item	*ŋ/*n > \it{n} (\trf{07-08});
	\item	weakening of *k;
	\item	*y is lost with traces in vowels in some items;
				(e.g. PMP *Raya ‘big’ > \it{e-hea} ‘big’;
				PCEMP **maya ‘tongue’ > \it{mea-ni});
	\item	*p > \it{f} (\trf{09-07});
	\item	**butu > \it{boro} ‘ten’.
\end{itemize}

with *t = \it{t} /\it{en-}{\gap} (lexically specific,
