\ssscp{Nasals}{07-04-01-01}
One of the clearest sound changes which sets \tsc{Ambon-Seram}
apart from neighbouring subgroups is the merger of *n/*ŋ > \it{n} in all positions.
This merger also occurs in Banda,
and it might point to a link between them at a higher level,
though there are many additional differences (itemised above)
that prevent us from taking this step.
Individual languages in \tsc{Sula-Buru}
and \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} also have *ŋ > \it{n} in all
positions, but *ŋ is retained unchanged or has outcomes different from *n
in other members of these groups.

Notably, most members of \tsc{East Seram} have medial *ŋ \it{>
k} -- despite these languages being in close proximity to \tsc{Ambon-Seram}.
These languages do have initial *ŋ > \it{n} (see reflexes of
*ŋajan ‘name’ and *ŋijuŋ ‘nose’ in \trf{07-12}) Denasalisation similar to that
of \tsc{East Seram} is also found in the languages of the Bomberai
peninsula, though in those languages it also happens word initially.
Examples of the reflexes of *ŋ across central Maluku are given in \trf{07-08}.
