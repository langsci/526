\ssscp{Basic vocabulary: ‘hair’ > ‘leaf of head’}{02-03-01-05}
One might think that basic vocabulary concepts such as ‘hair’
should be fairly stable.
Most languages west of the Blust line retain reflexes of PMP
*bulu ‘body hair, fur, feather’ (data in \qf{02-32} from \citealp{BlustTrussel2020}).

This same etymon is also found in many Austronesian languages
of eastern Indonesia and Timor-Leste, albeit following the word order associated with the preposed possessor.
(Data in \qf{02-33} are from Grimes fieldnotes,
Edwards fieldnotes.) Many languages of our target region have
a reflex of *bulu which refers exclusively to ‘body hair,
fur, feather’ and have a different mono-morphemic word for ‘head
hair’.\footnote{

Yet another pattern begins to be found in the Austronesian languages
in the region as well,
using terms meaning ‘leaf’ -- often reflexes of PMP *dahun ‘leaf’.
\citet[147-148]{Blust1993,DonohueGrimes2008}
and \citet{GrimesCIP} observe that in eastern Indonesia
and Timor-Leste and continuing further eastward into Oceania,
some Austronesian languages have shifted to ‘leaf of head = hair’.
This turns out to be a common Papuan pattern.
Note that in related languages on the same island,
we have both patterns.
Two Rote languages in \qf{02-33} retain reflexes of PMP *bulu,
and two in \qf{02-34} follow the Papuan pattern of ‘head leaf’.
Termanu (central Rote) can use either a reflex of *dahun ‘leaf’
or *bulu ‘hair’ for head hair,
with \citet[64]{Jonker1908} giving both phrases as equivalent.
Some of these languages,
such as Rikou, retain reflexes of *bulu for ‘animal fur’,
whereas others such as Dhao also use a reflex of *dahun ‘leaf’ for ‘animal fur’.
We note that collocation of ‘head leaf’ = ‘hair’ is also found
among Oceanic languages including: Fijian,
Tongan, Sāmoan, and Māori among others.

So following the logic spelled out by \citet[14--16]{Klamer2019},
it is reasonable to infer that the pattern ‘head leaf’ = ‘hair’
in Austronesian languages east of the Blust line is triggered
by contact with Papuan languages.
We note that its distribution is sporadic.
It does not replace PMP *bulu,
and does not cover all languages in any subgroup.
These factors mean we cannot use it for linguistic classification.
But it is also geographically widespread east of the Blust line
region persisting as far east as Polynesia.
It is a “contaminant” from the perspective of understanding Austronesian
dispersal, while at the same time being a very valuable trace of the intensity
of contact with Papuan languages,
even very far from where they are still spoken.
The distribution of known reflexes of ‘leaf of head’ = ‘hair’
in Papuan and Austronesian languages in the Wallacean region is shown in
\frf{02-18}.
