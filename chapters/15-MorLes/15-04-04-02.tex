\ssscp{Distribution of words for ‘tongue’}{15-04-04-02}
\frf{15-09} shows the distribution of known reflexes of
**maya ‘tongue’, and several other etyma
also meaning ‘tongue’ that persist in the region.

The variety of words for tongue is in many ways a reflection
of the complexity and diversity that we find in linguistic Wallacea for other things as well.
Reflexes of **maya are not found in most \tsc{Bima-Lembata} languages,
but (unlike **malip) it is found in Taliabu (a \tsc{Celebic} language).
Reflexes of **maya are not found in most \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai}
languages, where reflexes of **kəRi are prevalent.
Note in Tanimbar that Yamdena
and Fordata follow the STB pattern,
while Selaru, Makatian, and Seluwasan follow the pattern found
in other parts of \tsc{Timor-Babar},
consistent with their different subgrouping affiliations.
Reflexes of **maya are not found in \tsc{Aru} languages,
and we note that forms similar to those found in the \tsc{Aru}
subgroup are also found elsewhere.
The sporadic distribution and discontinuities of several of these
forms continue out to Oceanic languages all the way to Polynesia
with Proto-Polynesian *qalelo ‘tongue’.

In spite of the claim to be a “replacement innovation” in \citet[740]{Blust2013},
PCEMP **maya ‘tongue’ is not found through the whole CEMP region,
and is not a replacement innovation.
\citet{BlustTrussel2020} curiously assign some of the same forms in the
same “CMP” languages to both PCEMP **maya ‘tongue’
and to \tsc{PAn} *Səma ‘tongue’.
Forms such as Lio
and Palu{\Q}e \it{ləma} ‘tongue’ (and similar forms in many languages
throughout the \tsc{Bima-Lembata} subgroup) are more easily linked
to PMP *həma ‘tongue than to PCEMP **maya ‘tongue’.\footnote{
		Similar forms for ‘tongue’ with an \it{l}-onset seems to be fairly widely
		distributed, including in over 40 languages in Vanuatu.
		However, Malcolm Ross (pers.
		comm.) believes that the forms in Vanuatu have a separate explanation
		in their historical phonologies.
		We are simply observing their similarities of form
		and meaning.}
Indeed, it is not always straightforward to determine if a particular
form for ‘tongue’ is a derivative of **maya or *həma.
Thus, for instance, we have identified Taliabu \it{maa-n} ‘tongue’
as reflex of **maya (with irregular loss of medial **y [see \srf{08-01-02}]),
but we note that the restructuring of PMP *əpat > \it{ŋ-haaʔ}
‘four’ and *ənəm > \it{nooŋ} ‘six’
would also parallel *həma > \it{maa-n}.

Possible (but irregular) reflexes of PMP *dilaq ‘tongue,
lick’ may also be found in the region with lowering of *i (or
more likely related to P(W)MP *zəlaq ‘tongue’,\footnote{
		Forms such as \it{elo} and \it{lelo} are extremely common
		in languages of southeast Sulawesi (cf.
		\citealp[105]{Mead1999}), and are assigned to PMP *dilaq ‘tongue,
		lick’ in \citet{GreenhillGray2008}.}
or an alternate competing form
such as *ləlaw ‘tongue’),
such as Bima \it{lera} ‘tongue’ (\tsc{Bima-Lembata}); Liana-Seti
\it{lela-} ‘tongue’ (\tsc{Ambon-Seram}), Masiwang \it{lehaʔ} (*l > \it{l,
h}; STB), Kowiai \it{yera} ‘tongue’ (Kowiai *l > \it{r} is regular;
STB); SHWNG Taba \it{plelo},
Buli \it{papleo}, Sawai \it{pɛplɛ-o},
Biak \it{kaprer}, all the way out to Proto-Polynesian *qalelo ‘tongue’.
So consequently the story of ‘tongue’ east of the Blust line
is far more complex than simply **maya ‘tongue’ being a replacement
innovation for PMP *dilaq in the CEMP region \citep[740]{Blust2013}.

In addition to the proto forms in example \trf{15-08a} there are
additional reconstructions not listed here.
