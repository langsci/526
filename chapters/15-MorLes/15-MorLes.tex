\chapter{More lessons from Wallace}\label{ch:MorLes}
In \srf{02-01},
we introduced two biogeographical zones along with the transition
zone between them known as Wallacea.
We return to that here,
and briefly replicate some of the discussion.
There are two separated biogeographical regions (\it{Sundaland}
and \it{Sahulland}) with relatively shallow seas connecting islands
within each of them,
and relatively deep water between the two zones (see \frf{02-01}).
Wallacea is the transition zone between the two biogeographical
regions, and is a zone that requires multiple deep water crossings for
animals or humans to traverse (\citealp{Bellwood2007,Bellwood2019ed,OConnor2015,Oppenheimer2003}; see \frf{02-04}).
Wallacea includes flora and fauna from both Sundaland
and Sahulland, as well as flora
and fauna endemic in (native to,
unique to) the region (see \frf{15-01}).
Wallacea is where the Austronesians coming from the north or
west would have first encountered marsupials,
and developed a need to talk about them (\crf{ch:Mar}; \citealp{Blust1982,Blust2012b,Schapper2011}).

\begin{figure}[h]\centering
	\frame{\includegraphics[width=0.6\columnwidth]{15-01.jpg}}
	\fcp{Butterfly (\it{Troides prattorum}) said to be endemic on Buru}{15-01}
\end{figure}

Looking at this hypothetically,
but reflecting patterns observed by
Alfred Russel Wallace \citep{Wallace1869},
we start in Sahulland east of the \it{Lydekker Line}
on the island of New Guinea with 100 species of birds,
insects and animals associated with Sahulland.
As we travel westward the number of those 100 species becomes
fewer, sometimes gradually, and sometimes drastically as we move from
island group to island group.
When we reach or cross the \it{Wallace Line} travelling westward,
the number of species of fauna associated with Sahulland hits zero.
Specifically, west of the Wallace Line there are no more marsupials.

A similar thing happens travelling in the other direction,
but not so cleanly.
We start west of the Wallace Line in Java,
Bali, or Borneo with 100 species of birds,
insects and animals associated with Sundaland.
As we travel eastward the number of those 100 species becomes
fewer, sometimes gradually, and sometimes drastically.
When we reach or cross the \it{Lydekker Line} travelling eastward,
the number of species of fauna associated with Sundaland is significantly reduced.
More specifically, travelling from west to east into Wallacea,
the number of primates associated with Sundaland drops to zero
before reaching the Weber line.
And to complicate the picture,
we must not forget the many animals,
plants, birds, and insects (such as butterflies
and beetles) that are endemic to the transition zone of Wallacea,
some of which are quite unique,
such as the babirusa (\it{Babyrousa babyrussa}; see \frf{02-02}).

It is important to understand that the flora
and fauna are not distributed evenly across Wallacea.
And that there are some pockets that may be over-represented
with flora and fauna from one of the biogeographical zones,
and other pockets that are under-represented,
even within the overall trend of 100 species tapering down toward
zero while traversing in either direction.
