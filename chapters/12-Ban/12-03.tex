\scp{Labial stops}{12-03}
\citet[133]{Collins1986} observed “the treatment of the labial
stops distinguishes Banda from the descendants of Seran Laut”
with which he classified them under the label of “Proto-Banda”
(his Seran Laut ≈ our \tsc{Eastern Islands} node of \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai}).
Reflexes of *p for Banda are shown in example \qf{12-08}.
This shows the sound correspondences for Banda of *p- > {\0},
*-p- > {\0}, *-p = p (one instance).

Reflexes of *b for Banda are shown in example \qf{12-09},
which attests *b > \it{f}.
In the main text of \citet[131--134]{Collins1986} the reflex
of *b before \it{u} is transcribed as a bilabial fricative ‹ɸ›,
while in the word list in the appendix it is transcribed ‹f›.
This suggests allophony in which the labio-dental fricative is
realised as bilabial before /u/; that is /f/ → [ɸ] /{\gap}u.

The behaviour of PMP *b in Banda is not contrastive compared
with \tsc{Eastern Islands} and \tsc{Teor-Kur}.
Collins made his earlier observation based on comparing only
Banda, Geser, and Watubela, for which the reflexes of PMP *b are noticeably different.

While \tsc{Eastern Islands} and \tsc{Teor-Kur} languages consistently
retain a reflex of PMP *p in initial
and medial positions, Banda consistently loses it.
This could be seen as a secondary development,
but it does set Banda apart from those two nodes.
The development of *p in Banda is most closely paralleled by
\tsc{Timor-Babar} languages (\crf{ch:TB}), to the south of the Banda Islands,
which have initial and medial *p > \it{h} or *p > {\0},
while final *p = **p.
However, apart from this, there does not seem to be additional
evidence for placing Banda within \tsc{Timor-Babar}.
