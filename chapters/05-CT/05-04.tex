with *z > \it{s} being the most common reflex.

of *z > \it{r}.

PMP *b undergoes lenition in all \tsc{Central Timor} languages.
Kemak, \tsc{Mambae}, Liquiçá Tokodede
and Bazartete Tokodede have *b > \it{h} in all positions.
Some varieties of Kemak then have sporadic initial *h > {\0}.
Maubara Tokodede has *b > \it{v} in all positions.
Welaun has initial *b > \it{f}
and medial *b > \it{h}.
Examples are given in \trf{05-15}.
Two examples from Welaun which are not identical to Tetun
and establish the regularity of the pattern are *bəqbəq ‘mouth’
> \it{foha-t} ‘voice’ and *bubuŋ > \it{fuhuk} ‘ridgepole’.

PMP *w is usually retained unchanged word initially in Welaun
with one example of *w > \it{b} medially in *sawa > \it{saba} ‘snake’.

epenthesis of \it{h} /{\#}{\gap}VʔV (compare *ikuR > \it{hiʔo-r} ‘tail’).

Finally, Tokodede and some varieties of Kemak have loss of final
*n with compensatory lengthening of the previous vowel.
Some other varieties of Kemak have *n > \it{ŋ} syllable finally,
and this may represent an intermediate stage before loss of the nasal.
Thus, the full pathway may have been *Vn > **Vŋ > **Vː.
This process is attested in Tokodede, Marobo Kemak,
Lemia Kemak, Diirbatin Kemak, and Atsabe Kemak.
