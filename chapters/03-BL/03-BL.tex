\chapter{Bima-Lembata}\label{ch:BL}
\citet{Blust2008} observes that the purported Bima-Sumba group
has been assumed for 70 years or so,
without any claims for what it is based on.
Upon review he finds no support for it.
He proposes a new \tsc{Sumba-Havu} subgroup that excludes Bima
consisting of two branches: Sumba languages
and \tsc{Havu-Dhao} (based on an early draft of \citealt{Grimes2010a}).
At a higher level he links the west
and central Flores languages (represented by Manggarai)
to \tsc{Sumba-Havu}, though he does not demonstrate the validity of this group as
this is not his primary purpose.
At yet a higher level,
he tentatively links Bima to a group that includes both the Flores
languages and the languages of the former \tsc{Bima-Sumba} subgroup.
He does not define the scope or limits of this group
and does not explore what this group is based on.
Again, that is beyond his primary purpose.

We label this higher-level subgroup \tsc{Bima-Lembata},
which includes the former \tsc{Bima-Sumba}
and \tsc{Flores-Lembata} groups listed in earlier editions of the Ethnologue.
This higher-level group has lexical support from \cite{GrayGreenhill2009},
where it links directly to CEMP.
Similarly, \citet{Gasser2014b} also finds support for \tsc{Bima-Lembata}
based on Bayesian methods.

\citet[299ff]{Fricke2019} provides the first evidence from shared phonological
innovations in support of this group.
This evidence comes from the split of word-initial PMP *b > **β, **b.
Crucially, over a dozen cognate sets have so far been identified
which show lenition in all \tsc{Bima-Lembata} languages,
thus allowing us to posit *b > **β in these sets as a shared innovation.
Thirteen cognate sets which show lenition of *b in \tsc{Bima-Lembata}
are given in \trf{03-01}

While the first nine cognates sets in \trf{03-01}
and \trf{03-02} show lenition in all known \tsc{Bima-Lembata} words,
the final four: *bitiəs ‘calf’,
*buliR ‘grain-head’, *bəli ‘buy’
and *busuR ‘bow’, show apparent retention of *b as a plosive
in one language each.
In the case of *bitiəs the possible Waerana reflex \it{botes}
also shows irregular vowel reflexes
and may not be cognate.
Similarly, Alorese has \it{wuliŋ} {\tl} \it{buliŋ} ‘grain-head’
for which the second form may be a borrowing.
However, the words without lenition are not easily dismissed
for Sanggar *bəli > ‹\it{weli}› \it{{\tl} ‹mbeli}› ‘buy’,\footnote{
		Angle brackets indicate transcriptions which follow the source where
		the exact phonetic form is in doubt.
		The following issues, in particular,
		occur in Sanggar data: ‹e› may represent [ə] and/or [e],
		vowel sequences may have a medial glottal stop,
		‹d› and ‹b› may represent plain-voiced [d]
		and [b], imploded [ɗ]
		and [ɓ], and/or prenasalised [nd]
		and [mb] (the latter only in data from \citealt{Zollinger1850}).
		Zollinger’s ‹ng› represents both [ŋ] and [ŋg].
		We have converted Zollinger’s ‹è›
		and ‹é› to ‹e›,
		‹ò› to ‹o›, ‹oe› to ‹u›,
		‹ie› to ‹i›, ‹dj› to ‹ʤ›,
		and word-final ‹kh› to ‹ʔ› in all Sanggar forms not
		given in angle brackets.
		This is based on his use of these graphemes in the corresponding
		Malay and Bima word lists.}
and Ile Mandiri Lamaholot *busuR > \it{vuhuʔ} ‘bow’,
\it{buhuʔ} ‘clean and loosen cotton with a bow made of bamboo’.
For these two words we may need to reconstruct Proto-Bima-Lembata
*b.\footnote{
		A reviewer suggested that it may be better to reconstruct
		doublets *βəli/*mbəli and *βusur/*busur to Proto-Bima-Lembata to account for these
		variant forms.} 

While lenition of *b is not a particularly unusual sound change,
within the geographic context this change in a in a lexically specific set of words
does set \tsc{Bima-Lembata} apart from other subgroups.
While \tsc{Timor-Babar} languages also frequently also have lenition
of PMP *b, the retention of *b = \it{b} in Ilih East Damer (\srf{04-06-03})
and word-initial *b = \it{b} in Helong (\srf{04-01}),
means that such lenition cannot be posited at the level of Proto-Timor-Babar.
Similarly, while \tsc{Rote-Meto} has a split of initial *b- >
**f-, **b-, the changes in \tsc{Rote-Meto}
and \tsc{Bima-Lembata} affect different cognates sets.
Thus, for instance, Tii has initial /ɓ/ in \it{ɓatu} ‘stone’,
\it{ɓula-k} ‘moon’, \it{ɓafi} ‘pig’,
\it{ɓeli} ‘price’, \it{ɓafo-k} ‘upper surface’, \it{ɓifa ɗoo-k}
‘lips’, and \it{ɓulu-k} ‘body hair’, which can be compared with their
\tsc{Bima-Lembata} cognates in \trf{03-01}
and \trf{03-02} all of which have lenition of *b.

Examples in which initial *b does not undergo lenition in all \tsc{Bima-Lembata}
languages are given in \trf{03-03}
in which retentions of *b as a plosive are given in blue.
While some of these words have lenition in some branches and/or
languages, retention of *b as a stop in other branches indicates that lenition
in these words occurred after the break-up of Proto-Bima-Lembata.

The data indicates that *b > **β was probably a sound change
in progress in Proto-Bima-Lembata.
While it was complete in some cognate sets,
other cognate sets retained /b/ = [b],
and/or had allophonic variation with /b/ → [b] {\tl} [β].
After the break-up of the proto-language,
different nodes continued this sound change to different extents.

Lenition of *b often results in a merger with
PMP *w in many \tsc{Bima-Lembata} words.
However, the merger is not complete.
Havu and Dhao have *wa > {\0},
\it{o}; Bima has *wa > \it{wa, o}
and \tsc{West Central Flores} has *w = **w, {\0}.
Examples of the reflexes of PMP *w in select \tsc{Bima-Lembata}
languages are given in \trf{03-04}.

The internal relationships of \tsc{Bima-Lembata} languages are
reasonably complex, and are best represented with a wave diagram,
as given in \frf{03-02}.
Within \tsc{Bima-Lembata}, the following branches are well defined
on the basis of exclusively shared sound changes (not all sound
changes defining these branches are shown in \frf{03-02}): \tsc{West
Flores}, \tsc{Central Flores}, \tsc{Flores-Lembata}, and \tsc{Sumba-Havu}
(composed of \tsc{Sumba} and \tsc{Havu-Dhao}).
In addition, Kolo, Sanggar, and Bima share three sound changes
which allow them to be placed in an \tsc{East Sumbawa} group
(\srf{03-01}), though all the sound changes of this group are shared to differing
extents by other members of \tsc{Bima-Lembata}.

\begin{figure}
	\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{BL-wave.pdf}
	\fcp{\tsc{Bima-Lembata} wave diagram}{03-02}
\end{figure}

Apart from these well-defined branches,
two larger groupings within Flores can be proposed on the basis
of weaker evidence: Komodo,
\tsc{West Flores}, and \tsc{Central Flores} can be placed together
in a \tsc{West Central Flores} group,
and this group can be further placed with \tsc{Flores-Lembata}
in a larger \tsc{Flores} group.
