\chapter{Central Timor}\label{ch:CT}
The \tsc{\ili{Central} Timor} subgroup contains several languages of
central Timor: \ili{Welaun} [wlh],
\ili{Kemak} [kem], \ili{Tokodede} [tkd],
and the \tsc{Mambae} language/dialect cluster [mgm].
The locations of these languages are shown in \frf{05-01}.
Evidence for \tsc{\ili{Central} Timor} comes primarily from shared *ŋ > **g.
Supporting evidence comes from *R > {\0},
**r; *ma-p > **mp;
and *ə > \it{a} in final syllables.
These changes are discussed in more detail in \srf{05-02} below.

\end{figure}

All of these languages have had contact with \ili{Tetun} [tet] which
is socially dominant in central
and eastern Timor and has functioned as a regional lingua franca for centuries.
This function is now institutional in Timor-Leste,
as \ili{Tetun} Dili [tdt] -- a \ili{Tetun}-based creole -- is one of the official languages.

Among all \tsc{\ili{Central} Timor} languages,
\ili{Welaun} shows particularly strong signs of influence from \ili{Tetun}
[tet], specifically West \ili{Tetun} (\ili{Foho} \ili{Tetun}
and \ili{Fehan} \ili{Tetun}), which is spoken in the central Timor region
straddling the modern political border.
\ili{Welaun} shares many sound changes,
structural features, and lexical items with \ili{Tetun} to such an
extent that it is often impossible to determine whether a particular
lexical item is a native \ili{Welaun} term or a \ili{Tetun} loan.
\citet[35f]{Edwards2019} found that \ili{Welaun} showed more lexical
similarity to \ili{Tetun} than to languages of the \tsc{\ili{Central} Timor}
subgroup, with similarity between \ili{Welaun}
and \ili{Tetun} ranging from 55--58{\%}.
Indeed, nearly a third of \ili{Welaun} vocabulary on a basic word list
is identical to \ili{Tetun} (ignoring productive morphology).

In cases where a \ili{Welaun} word is formally
and semantically identical to \ili{Tetun},
but does not follow the regular \ili{Welaun} sound changes,
it is straightforward to identify it as a loan.
Thus, for instance, \ili{Welaun} *duha > \it{rua} ‘two’ does not show
regular *d > \it{l} (see \srf{05-02})
and is thus probably a \ili{Tetun} loan.
Similarly, \ili{Welaun} \it{nani} ‘swim’ does not show regular *ŋ >
**g > \it{k} (compare \ili{Kemak}
and \ili{Tokodede} \it{nagi} ‘swim’)
and is thus probably a \ili{Tetun} loan.

However, in cases where \ili{Welaun}
and \ili{Tetun} share the same sound changes, it is often impossible
to distinguish between inheritances and loans.
For instance, both \ili{Welaun}
and \ili{Tetun} have undergone initial *p > \it{h}.
There are at l\ili{east} two \ili{Welaun} words which are different to their
\ili{Tetun} equivalents and establish this as a sound change in \ili{Welaun}: *əpat > **pəat
> \it{hoat} ‘four’ and *pənuq > \it{honu} ‘full’
(\ili{Tetun} \it{haat} ‘four’ and \it{konu} ‘full’).
However, other words such as *pitu > \it{hitu} ‘seven’
and *puqun > \it{huun} ‘tree trunk,
source, base’ are identical in \ili{Welaun} and \ili{Tetun}.

There is no known strong phonological evidence for placing \tsc{\ili{Central}
Timor} with other languages of the region below the level of MP.
Evidence that \tsc{\ili{Central} Timor} is not a member of \tsc{Timor-Babar} -- its
closest Austronesian geographic neighbour -- comes from
the fact that *p > \it{h,} {\0} cannot be reconstructed to Proto-\ili{Central} Timor.
Instead, *p is retained unchanged in most words in \ili{Tokodede},
\ili{Kemak}, Northwest \ili{Mambae}, and some varieties of South \ili{Mambae}.
\ili{Central} \ili{Mambae} and other varieties of South \ili{Mambae} have *p > \it{f}.
\ili{Welaun} has initial *p > \it{h}.
(The reflex of word-medial *p in \ili{Welaun} is unclear due to lack
of cognates which are not identical to \ili{Tetun}.\footnote{
		There is only one putative reflex of PMP *p intervocalically which
		is not identical to West \ili{Tetun}: *tapi > \it{tabi} ‘winnow’
		(West \ili{Tetun} \it{tataʔek} ‘winnow’).
		This would show *p > \it{b} intervocalically.
		Other \ili{Welaun} words with reflexes of intervocalic *p are identical
		to West \ili{Tetun} cognates, from which they might be borrowed.
		West \ili{Tetun} has *p > **h > \it{ʔ} word medially.})
Examples are given in \trf{05-01}.

There are also a small number of words which show *p > \it{h}
in all \tsc{\ili{Central} Timor} languages.
One of these is \ili{Welaun},
\ili{Kemak}, and \ili{Central} \ili{Mambae} *pitu > \it{hitu} ‘seven’.
Another example is \it{mehi} ‘dream’
(ultimately from PMP *ma-hipi)
in several \ili{Kemak} varieties and South \ili{Mambae}.
That this is a borrowing from East \ili{Tetun} \it{mehi} (West \ili{Tetun}
\it{meʔi}) is indicated by \ili{Kutubaba} \ili{Kemak} \it{mə̆niip}
and \ili{Leosibe} \ili{Kemak} \it{mə̆nipi} from PMP *ma-h\<in\>ipi ‘dream’
in which medial *p is retained unchanged.\footnote{
		Throughout this chapter reduced ante-penultimate vowels in \ili{Kemak} are transcribed ‹ə̆›.
		Most such vowels are reflexes of reconstructed vowels,
		but the synchronic analysis of these vowels is unclear.
		They could be epenthetic insertions to break up consonant clusters,
		or they could be underlying vowels which are reduced due to being
		in pre-stressed ante-penultimate position.}
