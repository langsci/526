\chapter{Overview of central Maluku}\label{ch:CM}
This chapter provides background
and perspective on a number of issues that span subgroups in geographical central Maluku.
Much has been written about the region
and very few authors agree with each other on the classification of these languages.
Our examination of the historical phonology,
the morphosyntax, and the lexicon, lead us to different conclusions from other investigators.
That is why we present this overview chapter.
Primarily on the basis of the historical phonology,
we identify the following subgroups in geographical central Maluku:

\begin{itemize}
	\item	\tsc{Taliabu} (member of \tsc{Celebic}; \crf{ch:Tal})
	\item	\tsc{Sula-Buru} (\crf{ch:SB})
	\item	\tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} (\crf{ch:AS})
	\item	\tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} (\crf{ch:STB})
	\item	\ili{Banda} isolate (\crf{ch:Ban})
\end{itemize}


The location of these subgroups is shown in \frf{07-01},\footnote{
		\ili{Banda} is no longer spoken in the \ili{Banda} Islands,
		as indicated in \frf{07-01},
		but is spoken almost 400 km away in two refugee communities in
		the \ili{Kei} Islands (see \frf{11-02}) where they fled in the 17\su{th}
		century.} 
and the higher levels of the three biggest subgroups in this
region are given in \frf{07-02},
with fuller details found at the end of the chapters dealing with each subgroup.
\frf{07-03} shows the location of individual languages,
excluding the \tsc{Tanimbar-Bomberai} node of \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai}
and the modern location of \ili{Banda}.
See \frf{11-02} for the location of those languages.

Sociolinguistically, languages in geographical central Maluku
range from relatively large languages (for this region),
down to many languages or dialects spoken in only one or two villages.
Some are still spoken only by the older generation.
Some have become extinct in the last few decades,
and others are threatened -- some severely.
While a handful of languages are reasonably well documented,
overall the available data for the region are extremely limited,
and for some languages the most we have is a few words with transcription
problems and glossing issues in obscure 19\su{th} or early 20\su{th} century sources.

The largest languages in the region are: \ili{Kei} Kecil
and \ili{Kei} Besar (around 85,000 speakers combined),
\ili{Fordata} (around 50,000), \ili{Sanana}
and \ili{Mangole} (around 46,000 speakers combined); \citealp[89f]{Bloyd2020},
\ili{Buru} (around 45,000 speakers); \citealp{Grimes1991c,Grimes2000a},
\ili{Geser}-\ili{Gorom} (around 40,000),\footnote{
In this work data marked
specifically as “\ili{Geser}” is from \citet{Collins1986} and \citet{Stokhof1982a}.
Data marked as “\ili{Gorom}” is from \citet{Kakerissa1986} and \citet{Visser2023}.
Data which is identical in all of these sources is marked as
“\ili{Geser}-\ili{Gorom}”.}
\ili{Yamdena} (around 25,000),
\ili{Alune} (around 17,000), and North \ili{Wemale}
and South \ili{Wemale} (around 8,000 speakers combined).\footnote{
		The
		contact creole \ili{Ambon} \ili{Malay} would have significantly more speakers
		than any of these,
		particularly if one includes second-language speakers.}

Coastal villages of local people across the region are also interspersed
with villages full of migrants from Sulawesi
and north Halmahera who have been there for many generations.
In the southern coastal area of central Seram,
many villages moved from the mountainous interior in the past
150 years or so,
moving out of traditional language areas
and coming into intense contact with speakers of other immigrant languages.
In coastal \ili{Buru}, western Seram,
and the islands in between,
speakers of many indigenous languages were forcibly resettled
away from their traditional language areas to various Dutch forts
in the early to mid 1600s as a consequence of attempts to gain
monopolies in the spice trade.
These attempts included genocide in the \ili{Banda} islands.
The region of central Maluku has been heavily involved in the
spice trade for centuries,
and very likely for two millennia or more.
All of this together presents many challenges for unravelling
the prehistory of the languages in this region to a much greater
degree than in some other regions.\footnote{
		We found over 90
		“language” names cited in the literature on the region.
		\citet[1]{Stresemann1927} estimated there were 50 speech varieties
		on the island of Seram alone.
		While we have tried to resolve variant names,
		dialects of a common language,
		extinct languages, etc., some of these discrepancies have not
		been fully resolved in this volume.
		Some investigators use village names rather than language names,
		adding to the complexity.
		Some language names reported in the past are not known in modern locations.
		Some so-called “dialects” of the same language have different
		historical sound correspondences, noticeably different vocabulary, and reports of severely impeded
		intelligibility.}
(See \srf{02-02-01}; 
\citealp{Collins1982c,Collins2003b,
Florey1991,Grimes1994,Grimes2010b,
MusgraveEwing2006}).

While there has been a broad assumption that some of the languages
of geographical central Maluku group together in some way \citep{Stresemann1927,
Dyen1965,Dyen1978a,Chlenov1976,Collins1981,
Collins1982c,Collins1983,Collins1986}, there are different views on whether or
not the peripheral languages of the Sula archipelago (Indonesian:
\it{kepulauan Sula}, comprised of the islands of \ili{Taliabu},
\ili{Mangole} and Sulabesi) in the northwest,
and the languages of \ili{east} Seram
and offshore islands  in the \ili{east} should be included linguistically or not.
\ili{As} seen from the discussion in \srf{07-01} below,
there are also differing views between Stresemann,
Chlenov, Dyen, and Collins about how closely \ili{Buru}
and its related languages,
\ili{Ambon}-Lease languages, and Seram languages (west
and \ili{east}) relate to each other.
With the exception of \citet{Chlenov1976}
and \citet{LoskiLoski1989}, very few looking at the languages
of geographical central Maluku have explored the possibility
of linking those with the languages further south around \ili{Kei}
and Tanimbar, or further \ili{east} on the bird’s neck of west Papua.

Part of the reason for the conflicting views about the classification
of these languages is due to the complex set of partially overlapping
innovations which link the groups in this region together in a loose chain.
This chain can be visualised with a wave diagram.
\frf{07-04} is a simplified wave diagram of the main subgroups
within this region and \frf{07-05} is a more complete wave
diagram of subgroups and languages.
These diagrams only show sound changes.
The morphosyntax and lexicon present another set of partially
overlapping innovations, further adding to the complexity.

The remainder of this chapter proceeds as follows.
In \srf{07-01} we provide an overview of previous classifications
of the languages of this region.
We show that both \citet{Stresemann1927}
and \citet{Collins1981,Collins1983,
Collins1986} have proposed a “\ili{Central} Maluku” subgroup,
though in different configurations.
However, once the proposed innovations of each analyst are updated
according to a modern understanding of PMP proto-phonology,
we are left with very few,
if any, exclusively shared innovations in support of such a group.

In \srf{07-02} we discuss three general issues which contribute
to understanding the similarities
and differences between the languages of central Maluku.
We then discuss in \srf{07-03} the innovations which are shared
across multiple subgroups, while these innovations may
be suggestive of a single “\ili{Central}
Maluku” subgroup (consisting of \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram},
\tsc{Sula-Buru}, and parts of \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai}),
it would not be a strong one.
Most of the innovations are not strictly exclusive and,
as we show in \srf{07-04},
the patterned differences between these groups are significant,
suggesting that if they were ever together,
they split very early.

We then discuss the differences between the subgroups of central
Maluku in \srf{07-04} taking the central subgroup of \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram}
as our main point of comparison.
We show that these groups are distinguished by bundles of features
in the historical phonology,
the morphosyntax, and the lexicon in patterned ways from both
\tsc{Sula-Buru} to its west,
and \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} to its \ili{east} and south.
Overall, we find that the differences between these groups across
all domains are best accounted for by treating them as separate
subgroups rather than a single subgroup.
