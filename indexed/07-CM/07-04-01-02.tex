\ssscp{Apicals *d, *z, *R, *l, *j}{07-04-01-02}
Another feature which distinguishes \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} from neighbouring
subgroups is the behaviour of the apicals *d,
*z, *R, *l, and *j.
\tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} gives the strong impression of merging *R/*l/*j
> **l, while they are kept distinct in \tsc{Sula-Buru}
and \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} (with the exception of some
lower branches of \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai}).
There is some phonological conditioning in \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram}
languages, such that the reflexes of *l
and *R contiguous to [+LOW] vowel \it{a},
may be different than contiguous to [+HIGH] vowels \it{i, u}.
See \srf{10-01} and \citet[23--33]{Stresemann1927} for detailed
discussion of the different patterns observed in the data.
Yet like the behaviour of PMP *j,
different conditioned reflexes and lexically specific exceptions
can also be seen,
meaning *R and *l were not fully
and completely merged in \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram}.
The reflexes of the apicals *d/*z/*R/*l/*j in subgroups of central
Maluku and two nodes of \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} are summarised in \trf{07-09}.

\trf{07-10} shows the reflexes of *l in central Maluku languages.
(For more data see also *bulan in \trf{07-01},
*qaləjaw ‘day’ in \trf{07-05},
*layaR ‘sail’ in \trf{07-10},
and *təlu ‘three’ in \trf{07-14}.) The data show that while *l
= \it{l} is quite stable in \tsc{Sula-Buru} (other than a split
in \ili{Ambelau} to \it{l,
h}) and \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai}, the reflexes of *l in
\tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} have great diversity in the daughters.
One can also see that the reflexes of *l in \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram}
do not always align with the reflexes of *R in \trf{07-11}.
Conditioned reflexes of *l are underlined; underlining of a vowel
or vowels indicates a loss of *l.

\trf{07-11} shows reflexes of PMP *R,
with *R > **h in \tsc{Sula-Buru},
and *R > **l in \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram}.
(For additional reflexes of PMP *R,
see also *timuR ‘\ili{east} monsoon’ in \trf{07-02}.) \ili{As} in \trf{07-10},
keep in mind that \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} languages for the most part
have merged PMP apicals *d/*z/*R/*l/*j > **l,
and different reflexes in languages like \ili{Larike} are phonologically
conditioned by the vowels.
There is also assimilation at play in some languages when a word contains multiple liquids.
(Note: +t means the language appears to have retained the gloss
of ‘thorn’, alongside the more widespread gloss of ‘bone’ in this region.)
Conditioned reflexes of *R are underlined.

\trf{07-12} shows the lexically specific split of PMP *j > {\0}
in \tsc{Sula-Buru} and *j > **l in \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} in PMP *ŋajan ‘name’
and *huaji ‘younger sibling’.
Reflexes of *ŋijuŋ/*qijuŋ/*ujuŋ ‘nose’ also appear to show *j
> {\0} in \tsc{Sula-Buru} but *j > **l in \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram}.
However, it is not clear whether all the words in the ‘nose’
column in \trf{07-12} are from the same etymon.
The vowels of the \tsc{Sula-Buru} words are not regular from
any of the reconstructions.
Similarly, there are irregularities in the medial consonants
in the \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} words which point to additional complexities
in this cognate set.
Some \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} words with medial \it{n} may be explicable
as progressive assimilation whereby **l > \it{n} /nV{\gap}.

 In relation to the complexities in \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram},
note that the retentions of *j are not the same in these three
words for several of the languages,
making broad generalisations about splits
and mergers in \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} difficult.
Conditioned reflexes of *j are underlined.
