\chapter{Aru}\label{ch:Aru}
In this chapter we discuss the position
and internal subgrouping of the \tsc{Aru} languages.
For this, we rely almost entirely on \citet{Nivens2017},
a database of over 900 Proto-\ili{Aru} reconstructions with supporting reflexes.
All data and Proto-\ili{Aru} reconstructions in this chapter are from
this database with some differences in the analysis of the Proto-\ili{Aru} phoneme inventory.
Any future citation of this chapter or its conclusions must also
include a reference to \citet{Nivens2017}.\footnote{
		We suggest
		the following citation format: “Grimes
		and Edwards, based on \citet{Nivens2017}, {\ldots}”}
\citet{Nivens2017}
compiles data from several sources including his own fieldwork
and fieldwork by other linguists, mainly: Jock Hughes (many languages), Benjamin T.
Daigle (\ili{Batuley}, \ili{Mariri}), and Antoinette Schapper (\ili{Ujir}).\footnote{
		None of the people mentioned here are responsible for any
		errors in the presentation of this data in this chapter.
		Neither do they necessarily agree with our interpretation of
		this data.} 
Another important source in understanding Proto-\ili{Aru} is \citet{Nivens1993},
a reconstruction of Proto-\ili{Aru} based on data from West \ili{Tarangan}, \ili{Dobel}, and \ili{Kola}.
\citet[127--133]{Collins1982c} also provides a classification of
the \tsc{Aru} languages and describes the sound changes in the villages from which he collected data.
\citet{Chlenov1976} also presents a classification of the \tsc{Aru}
languages, with a more detailed discussion
and presentation given in \citet{ChlenovChlenova2008a}.

A map of the \tsc{Aru} languages is given in \frf{06-01}. For
languages spoken in more than one village,
the village from which data in this chapter is usually drawn is also indicated with a red dot.
All data which is not from these villages is explicitly flagged
in the table notes.
\ili{Karey}, \ili{Lorang}, \ili{Mariri}, and \ili{Kompane} are only spoken in one village each.

Transcriptions of data are adapted to IPA standards,
with the following exceptions.
The letter ‹y› is used for the palatal glide [j],
\tsc{Tarangan} mid-close [e] and [o] are transcribed ‹ê›
and ‹ô› (mid-open [ɛ]
and [ɔ] are transcribed ‹ɛ›
and ‹ɔ›), and unexpected stress is marked by an acute accent.
(The normal pattern is penultimate stress,
or final stress if the final syllable contains a mid-vowel.)
The symbol ‹ʍ› represents a voiceless labio-velar fricative.

\citet{GrayGreenhill2009} subgroup the \tsc{Aru} languages together with the languages
of “\ili{Central} Maluku” based on a Bayesian phylogenetic analysis
of the lexicon, albeit with marginal support.
Looking at both the lexicons
and historical phonologies of our \tsc{Sula-Buru},
\tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram}, and \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} subgroups,
we cannot support such a link.
Furthermore, while the languages in these three subgroups of
geographical central Maluku overall are fairly conservative in
retaining stable and widespread PMP vocabulary
and phonotactic structure, the \tsc{Aru} languages are not.

Regarding the lexicons, \citet[245]{Blust1993} observes,
“Almost all CMP languages apart from those of the \ili{Aru} Islands
are lexically quite conservative.” In the \tsc{Aru} languages,
several basic numerals, some common body parts,
and common verbs often do not reflect the PMP words widespread in nearby regions.
A sample of replacement innovations in the basic vocabulary in
\tsc{Aru} languages is given in \trf{06-01} to illustrate their lexical divergence.
The source of such lexical replacements is currently unknown.

In addition to lexical replacements,
the \tsc{Aru} languages often do not follow the lexical semantic
patterns which are widespread in the region.
Thus, for instance, many languages of the region have shifted
PMP *duRi ‘thorn’ > ‘bone’,
while \tsc{Aru} languages are conservative in having reflexes
of PMP *tuqəlan > **tul- for ‘bone’,
while Proto-\ili{Aru} **ruRi retains the meaning ‘thorn’.

\ili{Aru} languages are also different from the languages of central
Maluku in their historical phonologies.
\tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} languages tend to merge *d/*z/*j -- but not fully.
\tsc{Aru} languages keep the three distinct, but merge *j/*R.
\tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} languages tend to merge *n/*ñ/*ŋ -- but not fully.
\tsc{Aru} languages keep them distinct.
\tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} languages tend to merge *R/*l -- but not fully.
\tsc{Aru} languages keep them distinct.

The \tsc{Aru} languages have undergone many sound changes involving
both conditioned and unconditioned splits, with original conditioning environments
often erased by subsequent changes.
The complexity of the data is well exemplified by \citet{Blust2014}
who examines the historical phonology of \ili{Dobel} (based on the
data in \citealp{Hughes1995}).
Recurrent glide insertion, glide fortition,
and unstressed vowel loss have combined to obscure many regular Austronesian inheritances.
Thus, for instance, \ili{Dobel} \it{say} ‘fire’ is regular from PMP
*hapuy ‘fire’ via several intermediate stages: *hapuy > **api
> **ai > **yai > **ʤai > **sai > \it{say}.
