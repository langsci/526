\sscp{\tsc{\ili{Teun}-\ili{Nila}-Serua}}{04-06-04}
\largerpage
The \ili{Teun}, \ili{Nila} and \ili{Serua} languages are each spoken on three islands
of the same names to the north\ili{east} of \ili{Damer} Island.\footnote{
		The linguistic situation in \ili{Teun},
		\ili{Nila}, and \ili{Serua} was actually more complex.
		\ili{Teun} was also spoken in the village of Bumei on \ili{Nila} Island.
		There were also two \ili{Wetan} speaking villages,
		Isu and Layeni, in southeastern \ili{Teun} Island.
		\citet{Chlenova2006} provides data for this \ili{Wetan} variety.
		\citet[52, 78]{vanEngelenhoven2003}, who appears to have worked
		only with \ili{Serua} consultants,
		reports that \ili{Serua} was the lingua franca used between inhabitants
		of each island and on sea voyages,
		and that \ili{Serua} was spoken in Sifluru
		and Wotay in south \ili{Nila}.
		Edwards’ \ili{Nila} consultants reported that \ili{Nila} was spoken in all
		villages of \ili{Nila} Island (except Bumei; \ili{Teun} speaking).
		Edwards’ data from Wotay shows that this is indeed the \ili{Nila} language,
		with only slight differences from that in \citet{Taber1993}.}
From 1976--1983 the speakers were relocated to southern Seram,
but there are also reports as early as \citet[404]{Taber1993}
that some speakers have moved back to their home islands.
\citet{Edwards2023} has 505-item word lists for each language.
Apart from this, \citet{Taber1993} provides 193-item word lists
for all three languages.\footnote{
		There is a Holle list collected
		in 1937 and published in \citet[161--174]{Stokhof1981b} labelled “Niala”
		which is “from the village of Boemei [Bumei]”.
		Comparison of this list with other \ili{Teun} data shows that this
		list represents the \ili{Teun} language,
		which was indeed spoken in Bumei village on \ili{Nila} Island (see the preceding footnote).
		This list was reportedly collected in “Latoerake [\ili{Laturake}],
		Mountains in the \ili{Riring} region, West-Ceram”.
		The origins of this list represent an unresolved puzzle.
		\citet[197]{Collins1982d} states regarding this list (based on
		the limited metadata which accompanies it): “The informant,
		Matheus Soeplatoe, was a native speaker of \ili{Alune}; he was the
		head of a village near \ili{Riring},
		the military garrison and prison of West Seram in the colonial period.
		[{\ldots}] How Bpk Matheus learned \ili{Nila} [sic.] remains a puzzle.
		He may have served as a soldier or catechist there or he may
		have learned the language through a prisoner in the forced labour
		camp in \ili{Riring}.”}
A 500-item word list for \ili{Serua} is provided by \citet{Chlenova2004}.
\citet{vanEngelenhoven2003} provides some grammatical notes on \ili{Serua}.
Data is from \citet{Edwards2023}, unless otherwise noted.
This data is representative of the following villages: Yafila
(\ili{Teun}), Wotay (\ili{Nila}), and Lesluru (\ili{Serua}).

\citet[404f]{Taber1993} places \ili{Teun}, \ili{Nila},
and \ili{Serua} in a single group based on lexical similarity with
\ili{Nila} and \ili{Serua} closer to one another than either is to \ili{Teun}.
\ili{Teun} averages 69{\%} similarity to \ili{Nila}
and 66{\%} similarity to \ili{Serua},
while \ili{Nila} and \ili{Serua} average 76{\%} similarity.
Phonological support for placing these languages together comes
from shared *z > \it{s},
as shown in \trf{04-34}.

Additional support for \tsc{\ili{Teun}-\ili{Nila}-Serua} (TNS) comes from
shared *aw > \it{a} (\trf{04-35}) and,
perhaps, *R/*j > \it{r} (see below).
However, both these changes are also found in \tsc{\ili{Kisar}-Luang},
and East \ili{Damer} and may instead provide evidence for a higher
level node containing these groups.
Additional support for TNS,
which separates it from other languages of \tsc{Southwest Maluku},
comes from shared *ŋ > \it{k} in *laŋit > \it{lakti} ‘sky’ which
is unique to TNS.

Within TNS, there is evidence for a \tsc{\ili{Teun}-Nila} node on the basis
of shared phonological changes.
This conflicts with the evidence from lexical similarity which
places \ili{Nila} and \ili{Serua} together.
The first two changes shared by \ili{Teun}
and \ili{Nila} are *ay > \it{a},
and *b > **f.
The variety of \ili{Nila} in \citet{Taber1993},
from Kokroman village (represented as “\ili{Nila} (K)”),
has subsequent initial **f > \it{f,
h} and intervocalic **f > \it{h.} (The split of initial *b- >
**f- > \it{h-, f-} in Kokroman \ili{Nila} is roughly even in \citet{Taber1993} with
about half a dozen instances each.) \ili{Serua},
on the other hand,
has *ay > \it{i}
and usually has *b > \it{w} with subsequent **w > {\0} /V{\gap}\it{u}.
Examples of *ay are given in \trf{04-35}
and examples of *b are given in \trf{04-36}.

A third change shared by \ili{Teun}
and \ili{Nila} is insertion of the glide \it{w} before vowel initial words.
This change is more complex than the other two,
and requires detailed discussion,
as well as consideration of the reflexes of PMP *w.
\ili{Teun} and \ili{Nila} have retention of *w = \it{w},
as well as insertion of \it{w} before most vowel initial words.
\ili{Serua}, on the other hand has no such insertion but has also lost word-initial *w.
Examples of each change are given in \trf{04-37a}
and \trf{04-37b}.

There are logically three explanations for this data:

\begin{itemize}
	\item[i.] {\it{w} was inserted in \ili{Teun} and \ili{Nila}.
						\ili{Serua} has independent *w > {\0}.}
	\item[ii.] {*w was lost at the level of Proto-TNS.
							\ili{Teun} and \ili{Nila} subsequently inserted \it{w} before all vowel initial
							words, including those with earlier *w > {\0}.}
	\item[iii.] {Insertion of \it{w} occurred in Proto-TNS.
							\ili{Serua} subsequently had *w > {\0},
							which affected both PMP *w
							and newly acquired Proto-TNS **w.}
\end{itemize}

Careful examination of the data indicates that hypothesis
(i.) or (ii.) is correct
and that insertion of \it{w} only affected \ili{Teun} and \ili{Nila}.
It can thus be identified as an additional sound change shared
by these two languages.
Furthermore, the data indicates that this change either spread
by diffusion from \ili{Teun} into \ili{Nila} or was a change in progress
at the level of Proto-\ili{Teun}-\ili{Nila} which was then carried out to
a greater extent in \ili{Teun}.
The evidence for this scenario is twofold.

Firstly, \ili{Teun} also has insertion of \it{w} intervocalically before the vowel \it{a}.
Such \it{w} insertion also occurs after loss of medial consonants,
including cases of *R > **r > {\0}.
Examples are given in \trf{04-38}.

Secondly, \ili{Teun} has instances of word-initial glide insertion
in environments where \ili{Nila} does not.
The variety of \ili{Teun} (Mesa or Bumei village) in \citet{Taber1993}
usually has insertion of \it{y} before words with initial *i.\footnote{
		The equivalent \ili{Teun} words in \citet{Edwards2023} are \it{ina} ‘mother’,
		\it{hiru} ‘how much’, and \it{hitu} ‘seven’.
		Initial \it{h} appears to be an insertion after *p > {\0},
		as there are other words in Edwards’ data which have optional
		insertion of \it{h} /{\#}i{\gap}.
		Examples include *ia > \it{ii {\tl} hii} \tsc{‘3sg’},
		*kita > \it{ita {\tl} hita} \tsc{‘1pl.incl’},
		and *si-ida > \it{hira {\tl} ira {\tl} ir} \tsc{‘3pl’}.}
Examples are *ina > \ili{Teun} \it{yina},
\ili{Nila}, \ili{Serua} \it{ina} ‘mother’, *pija > **hija > \ili{Teun} \it{yiru},
\ili{Nila}, \ili{Serua} \it{ira} ‘how much’,
*pitu > \it{yitu}, \ili{Nila},
\ili{Serua} \it{itu} ‘seven’.\footnote{
		One word with initial \it{i}
		in \ili{Teun} which does not have glide insertion
		is *hikan > \it{ina} ‘fish’.}
There are also some words with initial vowels other
than \it{i} which have insertion of \it{y}: **ai > \ili{Teun} \it{yai},
\ili{Nila} \it{ai} ‘boat’ (\ili{Serua} \it{wroa} ‘boat’)
and **amat > \ili{Teun} \it{yamʔa},
\ili{Nila}, \ili{Serua} \it{amta} ‘shark’, as well as one word with initial
\it{i} which has insertion of \it{w} in \ili{Teun},
but not \ili{Nila}: *quliŋ > \ili{Teun} \it{willi},
\ili{Nila} \it{ilni}, \ili{Serua} \it{ini} ‘rudder’.

This data can be explained if glide insertion was a change which
only affected \ili{Teun} and \ili{Nila} and that it was more thorough
and continued in a greater range of environments in \ili{Teun} than \ili{Nila}.
There are thus three changes shared by \ili{Teun}
and \ili{Nila}: *ay > \it{i},
*b > **f, *{\0} > \it{w} /{\#}{\gap}V.

One change that sets \ili{Serua} apart from \ili{Teun}
and \ili{Nila} is *ŋ > \it{n},
{\0} while \ili{Teun} and \ili{Nila} have universal
*ŋ > \it{n}, though there
are only two clear examples of *ŋ > {\0}
in the available \ili{Serua} data.
Examples of *ŋ in \ili{Teun},
\ili{Nila}, and \ili{Serua} are given in \trf{04-39}.

The reflexes of *d,
*R, and *j provide another perspective on the internal relationships
of \ili{Teun}, \ili{Nila}, and \ili{Serua}.
In all three languages *d > \it{r}.
Examples are given in \trf{04-40}.
See also *dəpa ‘fathom’
and *daRaq ‘blood’ in \trf{04-38}.

PMP *j/*R > \it{r} in \ili{Nila} and \ili{Serua}.
Thus, these languages merge *d/*j/*R > \it{r}.
In \ili{Teun} *j and *R each undergo a split to {\0}
and \it{r}, without clear conditioning environments.
Examples of *j are given in \trf{04-41}.
Another possible example of *j is *bukij > \ili{Teun},
\ili{Nila} \it{fura}, \ili{Serua} \it{wura} ‘mountain’.

There are two solutions to the reflexes of *d,
*j, and *R in \tsc{\ili{Teun}-\ili{Nila}-Serua}.
Firstly, we can posit a merger of *d/*j/*R > \it{r} in \ili{Serua} and \ili{Nila}.
This would conflict with the sound changes unifying \ili{Teun}
and \ili{Nila} (*ay > \it{a,} *b > \it{f},
{\0} > \it{w} /{\#}{\gap}V),
but align with the lexical data in which \ili{Nila}
and \ili{Serua} are closer to one another than either is to \ili{Teun}.
Alternately, we can posit shared *R/*j > **r at the level of
Proto-TNS, which was followed by **r > {\0},
\it{r} in \ili{Teun}, after which *d > \it{r} spread by diffusion between the three languages.
These two solutions are outlined in \qf{04-24} and \qf{04-25} below.

{\wg\st{6pt}\tblr{>{\hspace{-\tabcolsep}}l<{\hspace{-\tabcolsep}}>{\hspace{-\tabcolsep}\enspace}lllll}
\tbx{04-24}	&	\mc{4}{l}{Solution 1}\\
						&a.	&*d/*R/*j	&>&\it{r}				&\ili{Nila} and \ili{Serua}\\
						&b.	&*R/*j		&>&{\0}, \it{r}	&\ili{Teun}\\
						&c.	&*d				&>&\it{r}				&\ili{Teun}\\
\tbx{04-25}	&	\mc{4}{l}{Solution 2}\\
						&a.	&*R/*j		&>&**r					&\ili{Teun}, \ili{Nila}, and \ili{Serua}\\
						&b.	&**r			&>&{\0}, \it{r}	&\ili{Teun}\\
						&c.	&*d				&>&\it{r}				&\ili{Teun}, \ili{Nila}, and \ili{Serua}\\
\end{tabular}\mbox{}\\}

Whichever solution to the reflexes of *d/*R/*j we adopt,
the lexical similarity and other sound changes indicate there
was a shared period of development and/or contact between \ili{Nila}
and \ili{Teun}, as well as \ili{Nila}
and \ili{Serua}, but not shared between \ili{Serua}
and \ili{Teun} (apart from \ili{Nila}).
This is unsurprising given the central location of \ili{Nila}
Island between \ili{Teun} and \ili{Serua} Islands (see \frf{04-01}.
