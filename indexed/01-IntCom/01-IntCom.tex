\chapter{Introduction to a complex region}\label{ch:IntCom}
The Austronesian languages of modern-day eastern Indonesia and Timor-Leste have long been the subject of debate.
At l\ili{east} as early as \citet{vanHoevell1877}, there began to be a growing awareness among some Dutch and
German scholars that:
(a) the languages in eastern Indonesia somehow differed from the Austronesian languages in western Indonesia,
(b) a growing awareness by some that these languages were all somehow related to languages in the Pacific,
particularly Polynesia.
But there was no consensus about (c) where the boundary between \ili{east} and
west might be,
(d) what the basis of the differences were, and
(e) whether the languages in the middle should group with the languages to the west,
those to the \ili{east},
or be seen as a transition zone between west and
\ili{east} (\frf{01-01}).

\begin{figure}[h]
	\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{west-east.pdf}
	\fcp{Spread of Austronesian languages, with west-\ili{east} division}{01-01}
\end{figure}

These 260 or so languages in the middle continue
to rank among the most poorly documented within Austronesia.\footnote{
		Views differ greatly among linguists as to what constitutes a “language” and what constitutes a “dialect”.
		Different historical sound correspondences,
		different grammatical functors, significant differences in the lexicon,
		along with reports of significantly hindered intelligibility
		make us lean towards treating two related varieties
		as separate languages where information is available.}
Their classification is also among the most contentious.
\citet{Blust2012b} acknowledged that scholars “are far from reaching universal agreement” on the classification of these languages.
It is these languages and
their classification which are the focus of this study.
