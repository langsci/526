\sscp{\tsc{Sumba-Havu} as a subgroup}{03-03-03}
\citet{Blust2008} proposes a \tsc{Sumba-Havu} node based on four
lexically specific phonological irregularities,
six instances of morphological fossilization,
and 97 shared lexical items.
Blust purposely limits his investigation to \ili{Kambera}
and \ili{Havu} as the best-described languages of this node.
Examination of other putative members of \tsc{Sumba-Havu} shows
that the proposed phonological irregularities\footnote{
		\citet[90]{Blust2008}
		proposes the following lexically specific phonological irregularities:
		(i.) Irregular *ə > **o in *qaləjaw > putative Proto-Sumba-\ili{Havu} (PSH) **loɗo.
		This cannot account for \ili{Mangili},
		\ili{Anakalang}, \ili{Mamboru} ‹làdo› [lɐɗo] or \ili{Laboya} \it{laɗo}.
		(ii.) *p > **ʔ in *dapuR > PSH **raʔu ‘hearth’.
		This cannot account for \ili{Wanukaka} \it{rapu} ‘hearth’.
		(iii.) Consonant metathesis in *qulun-an > PSH **nulaŋ ‘wooden headrest, pillow’.
		This cannot account for \ili{Weyewa} \it{luna}.
		(iv.) *añəm > PSH **uñaŋ.
		This cannot account for \ili{Anakalang} \it{wanaŋ-u},
		\ili{Mamboru}, \ili{Weyewa} \it{wanan-a}, \ili{Kodi} \it{ɣanaŋ-o},
		or \ili{Loli} \it{nane}.} 
and all but one of the morphological irregularities\footnote{
		\citet[91]{Blust2008}
		proposes the following morphological irregularities: (i.) PMP
		*kita ‘see’ > Proto-Sumba-\ili{Havu} **ŋita,
		for which Blust concedes both \it{ita}
		and \it{ŋita} occur for \ili{Kambera}.
		Putative **ŋita is further not known in \ili{Dhao} or other languages of Sumba.
		(ii.) *qunap > PSH **napi ‘fish scale’ has probable cognates
		in Rote, such as \ili{Termanu} \it{hani-k} ‘shell’ (with consonant metathesis from intermediate **pani).
		(iii.) PMP *di atas > PSH **diatas ‘above,
		on top’ has cognates in Flores (e.g.
		\ili{Central} Flores has **deta ‘above/mountain-wards’ -- see \trf{03-37}
		where Proto-Nuclear \ili{Central} Flores has **ʤeta).
		(iv.) *qəti > PSH **mənti ‘used up’ would be valid.
		(v.) *laRiw > PSH **pa-lai has cognates in \tsc{Flores-Lembata} (e.g.
		see reflexes \trf{03-46} \prf{tab:03-46})
		and Blust concedes that forms without initial *pa- occur in both \ili{Kambera} and \ili{Havu}.
		(vi.) *maŋ-kaən > PSH **ŋaŋ ‘eat’.
		\ili{As} Blust discusses, this form can be derived from PMP with loss
		of a morpheme boundary.
		It also does not account for \ili{Dhao} \it{-aʔa} ‘eat’.}
		do not account
for a wider range of data.
This would leave a single morphological innovation (*qəti > **mənti
‘used up, finished’) and the putative lexical innovations as evidence for \tsc{Sumba-Havu}.
Of the 97 putative lexical innovations,
Blust notes that eighteen have problematic vowel correspondences.
We further note that eight have cognates elsewhere,\footnote{

While we are sceptical of subgroups based on lexical evidence
(see \srf{15-04}),
it is worth investigating whether there is
additional evidence in favour of \tsc{Sumba-Havu}.
This subgroup is supported on the basis of at l\ili{east}
two shared sound changes: *R > **y > {\0} in a subset of words
and *j > **ɗ, **r (both identified by \citealp[93--94]{Blust2008}).
%\footnote{
		%\citet{Blust2008} identifies both *R > {\0}
		%and *d/*j > \it{r} for \ili{Kambera}
		%and \ili{Havu}, but appears to treat them as cases of parallel development (cf.
		%\citealp[93--94]{Blust2008}).}
Within \tsc{\ili{Bima}-Lembata}
*R > **y > {\0} is mostly unique,
though \tsc{East Sumbawa}, \ili{Komodo} (\srf{03-01-01}),
and some members of \tsc{West Lamaholot} (\srf{03-02-04}) have similar *R > {\0}.

\tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} have *R > {\0} in nearly all words,
while languages of Sumba have a split with *R > {\0}, \it{r}.
Nonetheless, there are eleven words with cognates in both Sumba
and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} which share *R > {\0}.
These words are given in \trf{03-51}.
Words which provide evidence of earlier **y are shaded.

The shaded words in \trf{03-51} show palatalisation or raising
of vowels adjacent to *R.
This provides indirect evidence that *R went through an intermediate
glide stage in at l\ili{east} some words.
This change -- loss with palatalisation of adjacent vowels ---
is consistent with the changes affecting PMP *y in Sumba and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao}.
Word medially PMP *y > \it{y,} {\0}[+fr] in Sumba
and *y > {\0} in \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao}.
Two examples of *y > {\0}[+fr] in Sumba are *daya ‘upstream’
>  \ili{Kambera},
\ili{Mangili}, \ili{Lewa} \it{ɗia}, \ili{Anakalang} \it{ɗee} (for which the changes
affecting the vowels are identical to those for *daRaq ‘blood’)
and *layaR ‘sail’ > \ili{Kambera},
\ili{Mangili}, \ili{Lewa}, \ili{Anakalang} \it{liru} ‘sail’, \ili{Mamboru},
\ili{Wanukaka} \it{leru} ‘sail’, \ili{Weyewa},
\ili{Kodi} \it{lere}, and \ili{Laboya} \it{leːro} (all with a historically epenthetic final vowel).
\citet[69]{Blust2008} also proposes that *R > {\0} in \ili{Havu} went
through intermediate **y due to otherwise unexpected *ə > \it{i}
in reflexes of *baqəRu ‘new’ > \ili{Havu} \it{viu,} \ili{Dhao} \it{hiu};
*dəŋəR ‘hear’ > \ili{Havu} \it{rəŋe {\tl} rəŋi} ‘hear', \ili{Dhao} \it{tadəŋi} ‘hear’;
and *bəRay ‘give’ > \ili{Havu} \it{vie}, \ili{Dhao} \it{hia}.

Some words with medial *R have a medial glottal stop in \ili{Weyewa},
\ili{Loli}, \ili{Laboya}, and \ili{Wanukaka}, which could be a reflex of *R.
However, these languages have a more general process of glottal stop insertion.
Examples include: *dahun > \ili{Weyewa},
\ili{Loli} \it{roʔo} ‘leaf’ (\ili{Kambera} \it{rau,
ruu}), *bayu > \ili{Weyewa}, \ili{Loli} \it{baʔi} ‘pound’ (\ili{Kambera} \it{ɓai}),
*kahu > \ili{Weyewa} \it{woʔu} ‘\tsc{2sg}’ (\ili{Loli} \it{wu,} \ili{Wanukaka}
\it{ou)}, and *daqan > \ili{Weyewa} \it{karaʔa} ‘branch’.
Thus, these medial glottal stops are not due to *R > \it{ʔ},
but rather due to *R > {\0} followed by {\0} > \it{ʔ} /V{\gap}V.

\ili{Kodi}, \ili{Anakalang}, \ili{Mamboru}, \ili{Mangili}, \ili{Havu},
