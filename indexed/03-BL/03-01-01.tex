\sscp{\tsc{East Sumbawa} and Komodo}{03-01-01}
\ili{As} mentioned above, the main changes which are shared between
\ili{Bima}, \ili{Kolo}, and \ili{Sanggar} also occur in \ili{Komodo},
as well as some other \tsc{\ili{Bima}-Lembata} languages.
These changes are *R > {\0}, * ay > \it{e,}
and *-aw > \it{o}.
In addition to the links between \tsc{East Sumbawa}
and \ili{Komodo}, there are also several changes exclusively shared
between \ili{Bima} and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} (\srf{03-03-03}).
The multiple links between \ili{Bima}
and other languages may be due to contact.
\ili{Bima} had considerable influence in the wider region,
particularly after the influence of the Sultan of Goa (Makassar)
waned from 1727 onwards.

The change *R > {\0} is shared by \tsc{East Sumbawa} and \ili{Komodo}.
Although \tsc{Sumba-Havu} often also has *R > {\0},
there is evidence that in \tsc{Sumba-Havu} *R merged with *y
after which it was lost.
That is \tsc{Sumba-Havu} usually has *R/*y > **y > {\0} (see \srf{03-03},
particularly \trf{03-51} on \prf{tab:03-51}).\footnote{
		There are also a handful of forms with *R = \it{r} in \tsc{Sumba}
		where it is lost in \tsc{East Sumbawa}; e.g.
		*paRih ‘stingray’, *uRat ‘vein’, and *baqəRu ‘new’ in \trf{03-09}.}
In \ili{Bima} *y > \it{ʤ}
and is thus distinct compared with *R > {\0}.
Examples of *y > \it{ʤ} in \ili{Bima} include: *bayu > \it{mbaʤu} ‘pound’,
*layaR > \it{loʤa} ‘sail’,
*duyuŋ > \it{ruʤu} ‘dugong’,
*kahiw > **kayu > \it{haʤu} ‘tree,
wood’, and *ma-həyaq > \it{maʤa} ‘ashamed,
shy’ \citep[80]{Blust2008}.\footnote{
		\ili{Havu} has one possible instance
		of PMP *y > \it{ʤ}; *duyuŋ > \it{ruʤu} ‘dugong’ \citep[332]{Jonker1908}.
		In other cases it has *y > {\0},
		often with fronting of an adjacent vowel.
		Two examples include *layaR > \it{lai} ‘sail’
		and *buqaya › \it{voe} ‘crocodile’.
		\tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} also has secondary **y > \it{ʄ}, \it{ʤ}, \it{y}.
		Examples include *kahiw > **kayu > \ili{Havu},
		\ili{Dhao} \it{aʄu} ‘wood’ and *i-aku > \ili{Havu} (\ili{Dimu},
		\ili{Raijua} Wawa) \it{ʄaa}, \ili{Havu} (\ili{Raijua} D{\Q}ida) \it{ʤoo},
		\ili{Havu} (\ili{Seba}, \ili{Liae}, \ili{Mehara}) \it{yaa}, \ili{Dhao} \it{ʤaʔa} \tsc{‘1sg’}.}
Thus, the changes affecting PMP *R in \tsc{East Sumbawa}
and \tsc{Sumba-Havu} cannot be taken as shared.

Examples of *R > {\0} in \tsc{East Sumbawa}
and \ili{Komodo} are given in \trf{03-09}, along with cognates from each of the other branches
of \tsc{\ili{Bima}-Lembata} for comparison.
While some words in \ili{Bima} appear to show *R > \it{ʔ},
this is most likely due to a latter process of intervocalic glottal stop insertion.
This is exemplified more clearly in words such as *niuR > \it{niʔu}
‘coconut’, *dahun > **roo > \it{roʔo} ‘leaf’,
and *qauR > **oo > \it{oʔo} ‘bamboo’.

There are also a handful of instances of *R > \it{r} in \tsc{East Sumbawa} and/or \ili{Komodo}.
Examples include *baRu > \ili{Sanggar},
\ili{Komodo} \it{waru} ‘sea hibiscus’ (\ili{Bima} \it{wau}),
*bəRəqat > \ili{Bima} \it{bara} ‘heavy’ (\ili{Sanggar} \it{dodo},
\ili{Komodo} \it{mədeh} [both non-cognate]),
and *təRas > \ili{Bima} \it{tera} ‘hard,
heartwood’, \ili{Kolo} ‹katòo› [katoʔo] ‘hard’ (\ili{Sanggar} ‹ka-ekh›
‘hard’ appears to be non-cognate).

The changes affecting final vowel-glide sequences *-ay
and *-aw are shared between \tsc{East Sumbawa},
\ili{Komodo}, \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao}, and -- in the case of *-aw > \it{o} -- \tsc{West Flores}.
\ili{Sika} and \ili{Kedang} also share *-ay > \it{e}
and *-aw > \it{o}.
But other \tsc{Flores-Lembata} languages have
a variety of reflexes which show that the full vowel-glide sequence
must be reconstructed for Proto-\ili{Bima}-Lembata.
\tsc{\ili{Central} Flores} also has sporadic examples of *-ay > \it{e}
and *-aw > \it{o}.
Examples of *-ay > \it{e}
and *-aw > \it{o} are given in \trf{03-11}
and \trf{03-12} respectively.

\ili{Bima} and \ili{Kolo} each have a split of *k > \it{h,
k}, {\0}, but in amongst the (limited) \ili{Kolo} data there are two
words which have differing reflexes compared with \ili{Bima}: *kədəŋ
> \ili{Kolo} \it{horu}, \ili{Bima} \it{kidi} ‘stand’ and *kaRat > \ili{Kolo} \it{kaka}, \ili{Bima} \it{haʔa} ‘bite’.
\ili{Bima} *k > \it{h,
k} also finds parallels in \ili{Komodo}.
Whenever \ili{Komodo} has medial *-k- > \it{h},
\ili{Bima} also has *k > \it{h}.
However, the reverse does not hold
and there are words with medial *k > \it{h} in \ili{Bima},
but *k = \it{k} in \ili{Komodo}.
Examples of medial *k are given in \trf{03-13}.
