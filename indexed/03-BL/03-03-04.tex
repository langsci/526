\sscp{\tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} and Bima}{03-03-04}
Before we conclude this chapter we need to point
out a possible connection between \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} and \ili{Bima}.
There are two (possibly four) changes that are
shared between \ili{Bima} and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao},
but not shared with \tsc{Sumba} or other \tsc{East Sumbawa} languages.
How these shared changes came about remains to be investigated.
It could be due to parallel development
(the position taken by \citealp[93f]{Blust2008}),
or it could be due to shared development, strengthened by contact with \ili{Bima},
given that \ili{Bima} historically had
the role of a dominant language.

The changes potentially shared between \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} and \ili{Bima} are
*t > **d, \it{t} (\ili{Dhao} **d > \it{ɖ͡ʐ}); *r > \it{r,
l}; *p > **f, \it{p} (\tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} **f > {\0});
and *s > \it{ʧ}, \it{s}.
Each of these changes involve splits,
and for all changes there are words which do not have the same
reflexes in \ili{Bima} vs. \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao}.
The different reflexes are summarised in \trf{03-15},
along with the number of reflexes in cognate sets for which a
reflex has been identified in both \ili{Bima} and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao}.

Positing shared development between \ili{Bima}
and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} for *p requires also positing *p > **f > {\0} in \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao}.
While this is a common sequence of changes,
there is no independent evidence that it occurred in \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao}.
Similarly, the change of *s > \it{ʧ} is only shared between \ili{Bima}
and \ili{Dhao}, as \ili{Havu} has undergone universal *s > \it{h}.
While some instances of *s > \it{h} may have been **ʧ at an earlier
stage, there is no independent evidence that this was the case.

Leaving aside the reflexes of *s,
while \ili{Bima} and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} do not always have the same reflexes,
the disagreements between \ili{Bima}
and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} are not random.\footnote{
		The discussion in \citet[93f]{Blust2008}
		is focussed on *p.
		%(Blust only mentions the changes affecting *l
		%and *t in passing).
		He concludes that, once conditioning environments are accounted
		for, the number of cases of agreement are insufficient to establish
		that the changes are not due to chance.
		However, if we leave aside shared retentions -- which
		would not be due to shared development -- the
		%it should be clear from \trf{03-15}
		number of cases of innovation in \trf{03-15}
		provide a basis for positing some kind of shared development.}
Whenever \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} *l has changed,
it has also changed in \ili{Bima},
while there is only one case in which \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} has *l
> \it{r} but \ili{Bima} retains *l = \it{l}.
Similarly, whenever *t and *p have changed in \ili{Bima},
they have also changed in \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao},
while there is only one case each in which \ili{Bima} is innovative
but \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} is conservative.
This means that we can posit a shared period of history between
\ili{Bima} and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} during which the changes *l > \it{r},
*t > \it{d}, and *p > **f occurred.
After this shared period of history,
\ili{Bima} had additional cases of *l > \it{r},
while \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} had additional cases of *t > \it{d}
and *p > **f > {\0}.

Given the proposed subgrouping in this chapter,
%links between \ili{Bima} and \tsc{East Sumbawa}
%on one hand, and the links between \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} and \tsc{Sumba}
%on the other hand,
the putative period of history shared
between \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} and \ili{Bima} was probably a period
of shared contact these languages had.
This contact would have taken place after
\ili{Bima} had split from Proto-East \ili{Sumbawa}
and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} had split from Proto-Sumba-\ili{Havu}.\footnote{
	Alternately, we could propose
	\ili{Bima} and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} subgroup together
	to the exclusion of \tsc{East Sumbawa} and \tsc{Sumba} respectively.
	This would then entail shared contact between \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao}
	and \tsc{Sumba} on the one hand, and \ili{Bima}
	and \tsc{East Sumbawa} on the other hand.}

Examples of shared *t > **d (> \it{ɖ͡ʐ} in \ili{Dhao}) in \ili{Bima}
and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} are given in \trf{03-16}
and \trf{03-17}, along with reflexes from \ili{Sanggar}
and \ili{Kambera} to show that this change is exclusive to \ili{Bima} and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao}.
In addition to these examples,
*qatay ‘liver’ (\trf{03-11})
and *batu ‘stone’ (\trf{03-01} also share *t > \it{d} in \ili{Bima} and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao}.

The six words which retain *t = \it{t} in \ili{Bima} for which a cognate
has been identified in \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} are given in \trf{03-18}.
Of these, three also retain *t = \it{t} in \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao},
and three have *t > \it{d} in \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao}.
The single word in which \ili{Bima} has *t > \it{d}
and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} have *t = \it{t} is *təktək ‘tokay gecko’
> \ili{Bima} \it{deke}, \ili{Havu} \it{teke}, \ili{Dhao} \it{təke}.
The onomatopoeic nature of this word may account for the irregularity.

Examples in which \ili{Bima} has *p > \it{f}
and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} has *p > {\0} are given in \trf{03-19},
and examples of *p = \it{p} are given in \trf{03-20}.
\citet[93]{Blust2008} observes that *p = \it{p} is the usual
reflex after schwa or in historic nasal-plosive clusters.
Two additional examples which show *p = \it{p} are *təpiR ‘mat’
(\trf{03-16}) and *pitu ‘seven’ (\trf{03-17}).

The two examples in which \ili{Bima} has *p = \it{p}
and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} has *p > {\0} are *paniŋ ‘feed’ > \ili{Bima} \it{pani},
\ili{Havu}, \ili{Dhao} \it{ani}, and *ma-nipis ‘thin’ > \ili{Bima} \it{nipi},
\ili{Havu} \it{menii}, \ili{Dhao} \it{manii}.
There is also one example which shows a split in \ili{Havu}: *qapəju
‘gall’ > \ili{Bima} \it{folu},
\ili{Havu} \it{əɗu} ‘gall’ alongside *qapəju > \ili{Havu} \it{pəɗu}, \ili{Dhao} \it{papədu} ‘bitter’.
The \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} words meaning ‘bitter’ are probably from
intermediate *ma-pəju > **mpəju,
thus attesting a historic nasal-plosive cluster.
There is only a single example in which \ili{Bima} has *p > \it{f}
and \tsc{\ili{Havu}-Dhao} has *p = \it{p}; *pahuq ‘mango’ > \ili{Bima} \it{foʔo},
\ili{Havu}, \ili{Dhao} \it{pau}.\footnote{
		One word with PMP *p which has
		a cognate in both \ili{Bima}
		and \ili{Havu}-\ili{Dhao} has an irregular reflex:
		*pənuq ‘full’ > \ili{Bima} \it{ɓini}, \ili{Dhao} \it{pənu}.
		\ili{Havu} has non-cognate \it{tobo} ‘full’.}

Among the splits potentially shared between \ili{Bima}
and \ili{Havu}-\ili{Dhao}, the split of *s > \it{ʧ,
s} provides the weakest evidence of being shared,
as this change is only unambiguously attested in \ili{Bima}
and \ili{Dhao}, as \ili{Havu} has *s > \it{h} in all cases.
Examples of *s = \it{s}
and *s > \it{ʧ} in \ili{Bima} are given in \trf{03-24}
and \trf{03-25} respectively.
\ili{Manggarai} reflexes are also given for comparison as it has regular *s > \it{ʧ}.
Note, however, that other languages of Flores,
with which \ili{Manggarai} is more closely related,
do not have *s > \it{ʧ}.
Thus, whatever the status of shared *s > \it{ʧ} in \ili{Bima}
and \ili{Dhao}, the change in \ili{Manggarai} may be due to parallel development.
