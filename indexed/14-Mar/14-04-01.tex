\sscp{Glosses associated with *mantəR}{14-04-01}
In \srf{02-04-04} we noted that \citet{Blust1982,Blust2012b}
had translated \it{buidelrat} from Dutch sources as ‘bandicoot’,
parsing it literally as ‘pouch rat’.
However several modern Dutch dictionaries give \ili{English} ‘possum,
opossum’ as the meaning associated with \it{buidelrat}.\footnote{
		For example, in the Dutch version of Wikipedia \it{buidelrat} redirects to
		\it{opossum} (Didelphidae) -- the family of marsupials found
		in the Americas. (‘Opossums’, Wikipedia. nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opossums.
		Accessed 22 June 2023)}
Possums are arboreal marsupials so close
in size, shape and behaviour to cuscus that they are often confused with
the cuscus where the two co-exist,
such as in northern Australia.
They both belong to the suborder Phalangeriformes which includes
“animals commonly known as possums,
gliders, and cuscus” (`Phalangeriformes', Wikipedia. en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phalangeriformes.
Accessed 22 June 2023).

We noted that \citet[304--305]{vanEkris1864} provides independent
evidence via \ili{Malay} that Dutch \it{buidelrat} was explicitly associated
with ‘cuscus’ during the era when many Dutch sources recorded
languages for which we have no other data.
For the \ili{Kamarian} language of southwest Seram,
van Ekris gives \it{maker} ‘buidelrat’,
\it{make mauriat} ‘gevlekte buidelrat [Ml.
kusu potar]’ (Ml. = \ili{Malay}), \it{make moon} ‘groote buidelrat’.
\it{Kusu} is the \ili{Ambon} \ili{Malay} term for ‘cuscus’.
And Dutch \it{gevlekte} = ‘spotted’,
so \it{make mauriat} ‘gevlekte buidelrat [Ml.
kusu potar]’ is explicitly identified in \ili{Malay} as a type of \it{kusu}
and refers to the ‘spotted cuscus (\it{Spilocuscus maculatus})’.
This is clear evidence that the term \it{buidelrat} in 19th century
Dutch sources can refer to ‘cuscus’.

Similarly, \citet{Stresemann1927} used German terms associated with northern
hemisphere animals to describe animals of Seram.
His Proto-\ili{Ambon} **maḏer is glossed interchangeably as both ‘Baummarder’ (e.g.
p.101) or ‘Beutelmarder’ (e.g. p. 129).
\it{Baummarder} = ‘marten, \it{Martes spp.}’ which is a nocturnal
tree-climbing animal, while \it{Beutelmarder} (literally ‘pouch marten’) is equated
by some modern sources with ‘quoll, \it{Dasyurus}’.
(e.g. ‘Beutelmarder’, Wikipedia. de.wikipedia.org/wiki/ Beutelmarder.
Accessed 22 June 2023).
Proto-\ili{Ambon} **maḏer is the etymon for \ili{Paulohi} \it{makel-e} (p. 41)
which is glossed in \citet[130]{Stresemann1918} as
``cuscus \it{Phalanger orientalis} and \it{Ph. maculatus}''.
Likewise, **maḏer is the etymon for
\ili{Nuaulu} \it{maran-e} (p. 101) and \ili{Kamarian} \it{maker} (p.
132), both of which are independently verified as ‘cuscus’ by
\citet[196f]{Ellen1972,Ellen1993} and \citet[304f]{vanEkris1864} respectively.
Furthermore, when the gloss of a reflex is different to his reconstruction
\citet{Stresemann1927} specifies the different gloss (such as on page 136
for \ili{Kobi}: “Mond SS. \it{umala} > \it{umel-am} „Sonne”.” indicating semantic shift
from Proto-Sub-Seram **umala ‘moon’ to \ili{Kobi} ‘sun’).
Therefore, all reflexes of Stresemann’s Proto-\ili{Ambon} **maḏer can
be taken to mean cuscus.\footnote{
		We have identified only one case in which Stresemann gives a different
		gloss to a reflex of **maḏer. This is \ili{Apiolo} \it{marel-e} which is
		specified as ``männl. Beutelmarder' = `male pouch marten' \citep[180]{Stresemann1927}.}

We also agree with \citet{Schapper2011} that \it{buidelrat} interpreted
as ‘bandicoot’ outside of or west of the region in which bandicoots
are known to inhabit is an unlikely interpretation.

There are additional challenges with the broad \ili{English} gloss
‘marsupial’, such as that found in \citet{Collins1983} for many cognates of **mans(aə)r.
\ili{English} ‘marsupial’ is a generic term that could refer to the
Phalanger cuscus, a tree kangaroo, a sugar glider, or a bandicoot.
But by extrapolation we have clear evidence that Collins uses
the gloss ‘marsupial’ to refer to the cuscus.
\citet[42]{Collins1981} gives the gloss ‘marsupial’ for the \ili{Buru} word \it{tonal} ‘cuscus’.
The \ili{Buru} word \it{tonal} refers to the cuscus
and not to any other animal (\cite[19]{Grimes1991c}, \cite[942]{GrimesGrimes2020}; see \frf{14-04}).
Furthermore tree kangaroos and bandicoots are not found as far west as \ili{Buru}.
In the same article \citet[36]{Collins1981} glosses \ili{Ambelau} \it{mate}
as ‘marsupial’, undoubtedly referring to the cuscus abundant on that island inhabited
by Muslims who do not hunt or eat it,
and using the same gloss he used for \ili{Buru} \it{tonal} in the same publication.
\citet[241]{Blust1982} incorrectly reglossed \ili{Ambelau} \it{mate}
as ‘bandicoot, marsupial rat’, apparently unaware that \ili{Ambelau} is far from the
region where bandicoots are known to be found.
\citet{BlustTrussel2020} now accept that \ili{Ambelau} \it{mate} should
be glossed ‘cuscus’.\footnote{
		\citet{BlustTrussel2020} incorrectly
		flag that the \ili{Ambelau} term involves metathesis.}

\citet[241]{Blust1982} also incorrectly glossed \ili{Asilulu} \it{marel}
as ‘bandicoot, marsupial rat’, again unaware that \ili{Ambon} is not in the region
where bandicoots are found,
with their known habitat not extending west of central Seram.
Regardless, \citet[70]{Collins2003a} provides the authoritative
gloss for \ili{Asilulu} \it{marel} as “phalanger,
including [\it{Phalanger orientalis orientalis Pallas}], [\it{Ph.
maculatus chrysorrhous Temminek}] and [\it{Ph.
celebensis Gray}]”.\footnote{
		The \ili{Asilulu} form with schwa \it{marəl}
		listed in \citet{BlustTrussel2020} is incorrect.
		\ili{Asilulu} has no schwa
		and there is no metathesis as indicated by Blust
		and Trussel (\ili{Asilulu} *nt/*nd/*ns > \it{r}; PMP *R > \it{l},
		as in *timuR > \ili{Asilulu} \it{timul} ‘\ili{east}’).}

Several of the words in other languages that \citet[55]{Collins1983}
etc.) glossed broadly as ‘marsupial’ are identified by
\citet{Ellen1972}[196f], \citeyear{Ellen1993} specifically as the Phalanger cuscus from data
that Ellen himself collected.

There is a practical problem working backwards as well.
What term was used by Collins
and others to elicit the words glossed with
the generic ‘marsupial’? For example,
the master Holle list \citep[50]{Stokhof1980a} equates item {\#}819
Dutch: \it{buideldier} `marsupial' (generic) = \ili{English}: \it{marsupial} (generic)
= Indonesian: \it{kuskus} ‘cuscus’ (specific).
There is no generic term in Indonesian for ‘marsupial’ that is
known by rural people in eastern Indonesia, even educated ones.
The Indonesian term \it{kuskus} ‘cuscus’ is known in many regions.
In parts of Maluku,
\ili{Ambon} \ili{Malay} \it{kusu} ‘cuscus’ is better known.
Some also associate the Indonesian word \it{tupai} (glossed in
dictionaries as ‘squirrel, tree shrew’ -- both animals that do not exist in eastern Indonesia)
with their local terms for the marsupial cuscus; e.g.
\citet[77]{LaidigLaidig1991} gloss \ili{Larike} \it{máridu} as: “tupai
[Melayu \ili{Ambon}: kusu]; \it{k.o.
marsupial, cuscus}”,\footnote{
		Similarly, John Christensen (pers. comm.
		18 October 2020) and David Mead (pers. comm.
		October 2020), report the gloss ‘tupai’ from their consultants
		for words meaning ‘cuscus’.}
equating one arboreal mammal with another.

The preceding paragraphs all provide background for the basis
for correcting several glosses of \ili{English} ‘marsupial’
and Dutch ‘buidelrat’ that have been incorrectly interpreted
as ‘bandicoot’, rather than ‘cuscus (possum, Phalanger)’.
This includes \citeauthor{Jonker1932}’s (\citeyear{Jonker1932}) disputed \it{buidelrat} for \ili{Leti}-\ili{Moa} \it{mada
{\tl} made}, which \citet[264]{Blust2012b} asserted must be ‘bandicoot’,
even though \citet[263]{Schapper2011} pointed out that \citet{vanEngelenhoven2004}
had listed \ili{Leti} \it{mada} as ‘cuscus’.
And as we noted,
\it{buidelrat} is glossed as ‘possum’ in several Dutch dictionaries,
and was explicitly identified as ‘cuscus’ by \citet{vanEkris1864}.
