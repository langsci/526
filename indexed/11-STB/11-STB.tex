\chapter{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai}\label{ch:STB}
In this chapter we discuss the relationships of the languages
of eastern Seram (and adjacent islands),
Tanimbar, \ili{Kei}, and the Austronesian languages of the Bomberai peninsula.
We find evidence for a \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} (STB) subgroup
which includes all of these languages,
except \ili{Makatian}, \ili{Seluwasan}, and \ili{Selaru} of Tanimbar which are
members of \tsc{Timor-Babar} (\crf{ch:TB}).
The locations of STB languages (along with \ili{Banda},
\crf{ch:Ban}) are shown in \frf{11-02}.
The higher level branches of STB are given in \frf{11-01},
and a wave diagram showing the main sound changes in \frf{11-00}.
A full classification is given at the end of the chapter.
The discussion in this chapter assumes an awareness of the broader
issues across the region discussed in \crf{ch:CM}.

Many of these languages are located in remote regions
and are severely under documented.
One language of eastern Seram,
\ili{Salas}, is currently unclassifiable due to lack of available data (\srf{07-05}).
On a very broad scale,
we note that the \tsc{East Seram}
and \tsc{Eastern Islands} nodes of STB share some features with
\tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} languages to the west (\crf{ch:AS}).

Note in particular, that we find evidence that \ili{Arguni},
\ili{Bedoanas}, \ili{Erokwanas}, \ili{Irarutu}, and \ili{Kuri} belong within in a single
\tsc{East Bomberai} node within the \tsc{Tanimbar-Bomberai} branch of STB.
The exact relation of these languages to one another
and to other Austronesian languages has been unclear,
and their classification has previously been elusive and disputed.
Thus, the proposal that these languages form a single lower level
node -- and that this node is linked with languages to the west -- is,
to the best of our knowledge,
new.\footnote{\label{fn:SchapperZobel2024}

Our \tsc{East Seram} node is equivalent to
\citeauthor{Collins1986}’ (\citeyear{Collins1986})
East Seram, though we do not include \tsc{Seti}
(placed within \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram}, \srf{10-03-04}).
Our \tsc{Eastern Islands} node is roughly equivalent
 to \citeauthor{Collins1986}’ (\citeyear{Collins1986})
“Proto-\ili{Banda}”, though we do not include \ili{Banda}
and do include \ili{Kowiai} (following \citealp{Zobel2014}).
Unlike \citet{Collins1986} we do not link these branches with \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram}.
See \srf{07-01-02} for fuller discussion of \citeauthor{Collins1986}’ classification.

Our \tsc{Tanimbar-Bomberai} branch is roughly
equivalent to \citeauthor{Blust1993}’s
\citeyear[276ff]{Blust1993} “\ili{Yamdena}-North Bomberai” which expanded Mills’ “Tanimbar-\ili{Kei}”
by adding the Bomberai languages \ili{Sekar},
\ili{Onin}, and \ili{Uruangnirin} (our \tsc{Bomberai Tip}).
\citet{Blust1993} also linked \ili{Arguni} with these languages.
Our \tsc{Tanimbar-Bomberai} node also includes the other Austronesian
languages of the Bomberai Peninsula: \ili{Bedoanas}, \ili{Erokwanas}, \ili{Irarutu}, and \ili{Kuri}.

STB as a whole is fairly conservative in its historical phonology,
with important retentions of *b = **b,
*p = **p, *ŋ = **ŋ,
*q = **q, penultimate *ə = **ə.
It also includes important witnesses such as \ili{Watubela} which preserves
reflexes of PMP *q > \it{k} in all positions,
as well as \ili{Geser}-\ili{Gorom}
and \ili{Yamdena} in which separate reflexes of *ma-b
and *ma-p preserve the nasal,
the vowel, and the labial.
\ili{Masiwang} is also an important witness (*R/*s > \it{s},
among other things) known in Dutch-era sources as “\ili{Bonfia}”,
although that name has not been used locally for many decades \citep[125]{Collins1986}.

\ili{Geser}-\ili{Gorom} is a large
and very dispersed language with internal variation,
spoken as far west as coastal areas of eastern Seram
and as far \ili{east} as the Bomberai Peninsula where it has had a
role as a language of wider communication.
Data labelled “\ili{Geser}” is from \citet{Collins1986} and \citet{Stokhof1982a}.
Data labelled “\ili{Gorom}” is from \citet{Kakerissa1986} and \citet{Visser2019a}.
Data which is identical in all of these sources is labelled “\ili{Geser}-\ili{Gorom}” or “\ili{Geser}-G.”.

\ili{Kei} (with around 86,000 speakers),
\ili{Fordata} (19,000 speakers), and \ili{Yamdena} (30,000 speakers) are
relatively large languages for the Maluku region.
\citet[102]{Hughes1987} notes that in the past,
\ili{Fordata} was also used throughout the Tanimbar Islands as a type
of lingua franca and as the language of customary law
and ritual, but no longer has those functions.

In \ili{Fordata}, stress is penultimate.
In \ili{Yamdena} stress is normally penultimate,
but can shift to the ultimate syllable with the addition of plural \it{-ar}.
In \ili{Kei} stress is ultimate.
Ed Travis (pers. comm.
November 1990) notes in the phonotactics that in the \ili{Kei} Besar
dialect of \ili{Kei} CV\sub{1}CV\sub{2} corresponds to CV\sub{1}C in the \ili{Kei} Kecil dialect.
Similarly, \ili{Kei} Besar CV\sub{1}V\sub{2}C > \ili{Kei} Kecil CV\sub{1}C.
Stress and/or tone is probably contrastive in some of the Bomberai
languages, though the most common pattern in our data is penultimate stress/high tone.

While the region encompassing STB languages is remote,
it has nevertheless been traversed by trade routes for centuries,
possibly as long as two millennia or more (see \srf{02-02}),
with some of the trade focused on \ili{Onin} on the Bomberai Peninsula.
Vocabulary across the subgroup is quite diverse.
A perusal of the word lists in \citet[141--146]{Collins1986}
shows great diversity in the vocabulary of six languages in the
northwestern parts of the subgroup,
though there also seem to be some vocabulary items which are
only found in STB languages,
such as **kəRi ‘tongue’,
**kakelan ‘chin, jaw’, and **putin ‘flower’.

A sample of the diversity within the group can be seen by comparing
the reflexes of *-uy in these languages, given in \trf{11-01}.
While we can posit a merger of other final vowel-glide sequences
(\srf{11-01-02}), the reflexes of *-uy require reconstruction of both the vowel
and glide to Proto-STB for *babuy ‘pig’ and *hapuy ‘fire’.
Reflexes of *laRiw ‘run,
flee’ are also shown in \trf{11-01} as space permits.
Although reflexes of *laRiw have not been identified in many
languages, what data is available indicates that the full vowel-glide sequence
probably also needs to be reconstructed for this word.
