\chapter{Banda}\label{ch:Ban}
The \ili{Banda} language is no longer spoken in the \ili{Banda} Islands located
about 140 km south of Seram,
but is still spoken in two separated remnant refugee communities
of \ili{Banda}-Elat and \ili{Banda}-Eli almost 400 km to the south\ili{east} of \ili{Banda} on the
island of \ili{Kei} Besar (see \frf{11-02}).
This move happened after the population of \ili{Banda} was decimated
by the Dutch in 1621,
and survivors fled their homeland \citep{CollinsKaartinen1998,Collins2003b}.
The eleven volcanic \ili{Banda} Islands in the \ili{Banda} Sea are isolated
enough to be the botanical source of nutmeg
and mace (\it{Myristica frangrans}),
and such geographical isolation may also have contributed to
the apparently unique linguistic development of the language.
Evidence of the presence of speakers of Austronesian languages
in the \ili{Banda} Islands dates to around 3,500 BP \citep{Spriggs2011,Lape2018}.

Out in the middle of the \ili{Banda} Sea,
the \ili{Banda} Islands have \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} languages to the north
and west (\crf{ch:AS}); \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} languages
to the north and \ili{east} (\crf{ch:STB}); \tsc{Aru} languages to the south\ili{east}
(\crf{ch:Aru}); and \tsc{Timor-Babar} languages to the south and southwest (\crf{ch:TB}).

\citet[182--190]{Stresemann1927} recognised that while the \ili{Banda}
language, which he called “Eli-Elat”,
shared some vocabulary with his Proto-\ili{Ambon} subgroup (roughly
our \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} plus parts of \tsc{Sula-Buru}; see \crf{ch:CM}),
it had a different phonological history,
preserving features of an earlier stage of linguistic development.
Unlike Proto-\ili{Ambon}, \ili{Banda} preserved historical final *k,
preserved final consonants on verbs that have been lost in Proto-\ili{Ambon},
fully preserved both parts of medial *NC clusters,
and had many other differences from the languages of Proto-\ili{Ambon}.
From a modern perspective,
however, many of those differences could reflect secondary developments
from a common source.
More importantly \citet[185, 188]{Stresemann1927}
also noticed different results of a merger of PMP *R/*d/*z
> \it{r} in \ili{Banda} from Proto-\ili{Ambon} *d/*z > **d,
and *R > \it{r}.
He also saw that \ili{Banda} had a different reflex of *ns > \it{ʤ},
from *nt/*nd/*nz/*nj > \it{d,
nd}, and thus did not share the merger of *nt/*nd/*nz/*nj/*ns
> **ḏ purportedly found in Proto-\ili{Ambon}.
However, perusal of his data shows that many of his forms are
derived from \ili{Malay} loans,
and others are from morphophonemic processes on inflected verbs
(see \trf{12-01}), so we have not yet found unambiguous examples that support his
distinctions from the historical phonology.
\citeauthor{Stresemann1927} also felt \ili{Banda} was distinct from his Proto-Goram
subgroup (roughly the \tsc{Eastern Islands} node of our \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} subgroup).

\citet{Chlenov1976} placed \ili{Banda} as its own branch of his “\ili{Ambon}”
subgroup, based mostly on lexicostatistics.
That separated it from \ili{Geser}
and \ili{Watubela}, which he placed in his “South Moluccan” subgroup.

\citet{Collins1986} argued that \ili{Banda} is classified in a branch
under the East \ili{Central} Maluku branch of his \ili{Central} Maluku subgroup,
and separate from his Nunusaku (in our terms,
not part of our \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} subgroup).
He grouped \ili{Banda} together with two languages (\ili{Geser}
and \ili{Watubela}) of our \tsc{Eastern Islands} node of \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai}
under his label “Proto-\ili{Banda}”,
partly based on shared mergers of PMP *d/*D with *z/*Z (which,
now recognised as retentions,
are no longer seen as having subgrouping value),
along with several other proposed splits
and mergers, some of which have exceptions in our data.
The strongest basis for “Proto-\ili{Banda}” was *R/*d/*z > \it{r} shared
between \ili{Banda} and \ili{Geser}, even though other parts of the historical phonology,
the vocabulary and morphology of those two languages appear to be quite different.
Collins himself (\citeyear{Collins1986}) documents many other ways in the
historical phonology that set \ili{Banda} apart from the other \tsc{Eastern
Islands} languages with which he grouped \ili{Banda}.

\citet[112]{LoskiLoski1989} treat \ili{Banda} as an isolate,
finding it is equally (un)related to languages in four different
nodes of our \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} subgroup,
based on lexical similarity
(see \crf{ch:CM}, footnote \ref{fn:BandaLexicalSimilarity},
\prf{fn:BandaLexicalSimilarity}).

In short, to date there has been no agreement among various investigators
regarding the classification of \ili{Banda}.
We treat \ili{Banda} as an isolate,
with this classification being tentative.
The innovations in the historical phonology we discuss in this
chapter are listed below.
While most of the patterns that are discussed in this chapter
individually do not provide a strong basis for separating \ili{Banda}
from \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} or \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai},
nevertheless put together as a bundle they present a fairly strong
case for treating \ili{Banda} as an isolate, pending additional data.

\begin{itemize}
		\item	The merger of nasals *n/*ŋ/*ñ > \it{n} in \ili{Banda} (\srf{12-01})
					is shared with \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram}.
					However, this is not exclusive,
					and the collective differences in the historical phonology,
					morphosyntax, and the lexicon lead us to conclude that this
					common merger very likely happened through parallel development,
					and is not an exclusively shared innovation defining a larger
					subgroup that includes \ili{Banda}.
		\item	In the apicals,
					\ili{Banda} merges *d/*z/*R > \it{r}
					and *j/*l > \it{l} in ways that contrast with the patterns of
					mergers of apicals in both \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} and \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} (\srf{12-02}).
					At a higher level,
					it may be possible to link \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram},
					\tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai}, and \ili{Banda} together on the basis
					of shared merger of *d/*z (\srf{07-03-03}, \srf{13-04}).
		\item	In the labials,
					\citet[133]{Collins1986} observed “the treatment of the labial
					stops distinguishes \ili{Banda} from the descendants of Seran Laut”
					(his Seran Laut ≈ our \tsc{Eastern Islands} node of \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai}).
					This is discussed in \srf{12-03}.
		\item	\citet[133f]{Collins1986} observed that reflexes of initial
					(*ma-C) and other initial and medial *NC clusters also distinguishes
					\ili{Banda} from “Seran Laut” (\srf{12-04}).
		\item	In the vowels,
					\ili{Banda} has *-ay/*-aw > \it{a},
					but final *a shows alternations of \it{a {\tl} o} which are not
					attested with reflexes of *-ay/*-aw,
					indicating that we cannot posit a straightforward merger of *-ay/*-aw/*-a in \ili{Banda} (\srf{12-05}).
					This pattern contrasts with both \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} and \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai}.
					\tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} does not merge *-ay/*-aw > \it{a} (see \srf{10-02}),
					and neither subgroup shows the alternations of final *a found in \ili{Banda}.
		\item	Also in the vowels,
					\ili{Banda} has penultimate *ə > \it{e}
					and a split of ultimate *ə > \it{e,
					o} as conditioned by the penultimate vowel (\srf{12-05}).
					It is possible to posit a partial merger of *ə/*a > \it{o} in
					final syllables, though there is evidence that this could not have gone through intermediate **a.
					This behaviour of ultimate *ə distinguishes \ili{Banda} from \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai}
					in which ultimate *ə > \it{a}.
		\item	\ili{Banda} preserves PMP *k = \it{k} in all word positions in all identified reflexes.
					This distinguishes it from \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} languages which
					generally show a weakening or loss of *k > \it{ʔ},
					{\0}, except in the morphophonemic processes of verb inflections in some languages.
		\item	The \tsc{Eastern Islands} node of \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai}
					preserves PMP *q in all positions (see \srf{11-03-01}).
					\ili{Banda} loses PMP *q > {\0} in all positions.
					If they shared a common parent language,
					this loss could be from a secondary development for \ili{Banda}.
\end{itemize}

In the morphosyntax,
like \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} languages, \ili{Banda} has multiple inflectional
paradigms in its verbs,
as exemplified in \trf{12-01}.
Unlike \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} languages, \ili{Banda} is not known to make the human/non-human distinction.

Also in the morphosyntax,
\ili{Banda} has two noun class distinctions of alienable
and inalienable nouns, reflected in two different possessive
constructions \citep[535f]{CollinsKaartinen1998}.
Such distinctions are also found in \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram}
and \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} languages, but not \tsc{Sula-Buru} (see \srf{02-03-01-01}).

In the lexicon, \ili{Banda} \it{meliri} ‘stand’ is from PMP *diRi,
like most (but not all) \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} languages (see \trf{11-02}).
This contrasts with \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} languages that take their
word for ‘stand’ from PMP *kədəŋ (see \trf{07-06}).
Since both are retentions,
this has no real subgrouping value for \ili{Banda}.
Note that \ili{Banda} *diRi > \it{meliri} ‘stand’ has irregular *d
> \it{l} in \ili{Banda}.
While we might want to posit regressive dissimilation,
this does not occur in other cases of *dVRV; *daRaq
> \it{raro-} ‘blood’, *daRəq > \it{raro} ‘clay’,
*duRi > \it{riri} ‘bone’.

Like \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} languages, \ili{Banda} \it{utuɲa} ‘hundred’
is derived from PCM **utum,
whereas \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} languages derive ‘hundred’
from PMP *Ratus (see \trf{07-06}).
Like \tsc{Sula-Buru} and \tsc{\ili{Ambon}-Seram} languages, \ili{Banda} \it{lea}
‘sun’ reflects a lexically specific loss of PMP *j in *qaləjaw ‘sun, day’.
PMP *j is mostly preserved in \tsc{Seram-Tanimbar-Bomberai} languages
in this etymon (see \trf{07-05}).
Almost uniquely in the region,
\ili{Banda} gets its word \it{tilu-} ‘ear’ from PMP *tilu/*tuli ‘earwax’,
rather than from *taliŋa or *taŋila as found
in neighbouring subgroups and languages.
